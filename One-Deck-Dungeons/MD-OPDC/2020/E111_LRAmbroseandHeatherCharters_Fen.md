**[Header]**
**F E N**

So, you have found yourselves in the Fen. This is a dangerous place even for the locals. Keep to the narroways and, when the mists thicken, do your best to find higher ground.

The Fen is a pocket dimension beneath the mortal world, an eternal marsh dribbling out in all directions. It is a place of peat fires, ancient willows, and a constant creeping mist. There are many ways in but only one way out.

**The Mists:** Every in-game hour roll 1d6 to determine if the mists thicken. **1-2:** the mists recede. **3-4:** the mists are unchanged. **5-6:** the mists thicken.

**The Fen Stalker:** There is a creature that keeps to the mists known as the Fen Stalker. When the mist is thin you may hear its howl. As they thicken it comes closer. In a white out, the Stalker strikes. To kill it only grants a reprieve, as it will re-emerge from the bog the following night.

**The Stilt Town:** A wandering burgh. The houses creep through the Fen on many insectile legs. Townfolk are superstitious and wary of outsiders. Roll on the below table to determine the town’s mood.

**Escaping the Fen:** The only way out of the Fen is through the Clearwater Spring. To find it, the Party must first find the Three Sisters, a set of standing stones scattered across the Fen. Triangulate the Sisters and the Spring will lie at its centre. Swimming to the bottom of the Spring returns the Party to a random pool in the mortal world. Most inhabitants of the Fen know of the Spring but will only tell the Party in exchange for gifts.

**Creatures of the Fen**
*   **Fretlings:** A variety of overgrown midges that fly in dense swarms. Born to annoy, harass, and whittle the minds of unfortunate Fen-walkers. Fretlings love blood and honey but hate smoke.
*   **Lamp Stringer:** The lantern fish of the Fen. They raise their lanterns as the mist thickens, drawing walkers deeper into the wet.
*   **Orogolutz:** Otherwise known as the Stepping-Stone Troll. These stony-hided beasts sleep face down in bog water. Beware to the traveller who sees a secure and inviting set of stepping-stones through the marsh.
*   **Sliveknick:** Vicious, knife-wielding toads. They hide in tree stumps, fallen logs, under rocks. Will stab your ankles when you’re not looking. When it rains, they gather by fallen trees to dance and display their loot.
*   **Tottkins:** These mostly harmless sprites resemble tufts of emerald green grass until they smell a campfire. Tottkins will eat unguarded food and throw belongings into the depths of the Fen. Their god, TottkinTottkan, is a hoarder of all things that glitter.
*   **Skumpi:** You will smell them before you see them. When a peat bog gets to a certain age, thoughts begin to ferment in its oozy mass. Toss a coin into its sludge and listen to its wafts. You never know what sages are pickled within who might offer a word of advice.

### Map Description (for gameplay purposes)

There is no traditional grid-based map provided on this page. Instead, the "Fen" acts as an **abstract, narrative-driven map**. If you are running this for a player who cannot see the page, you can treat it as a point-crawl or a hex-crawl with the following environmental logic:

1.  **Navigational Logic:** The environment is fluid. Directionality is not fixed to North, South, East, or West. Instead, navigation is determined by the **"Mists"** mechanic. When the mists are thick, players are lost and should look for "higher ground" or the "narroways." 
2.  **Key Landmarks:** 
    *   **The Three Sisters:** These act as the primary navigation beacons. They are standing stones, and locating all three is the win-condition for escaping. They should be described as massive, weathered rocks jutting out of the muck.
    *   **Clearwater Spring:** This is the center point between the three stones. Once the stones are found, the Spring represents the exit portal.
    *   **The Stilt Town:** This is a mobile landmark. It does not stay in one place, so it can be introduced by the DM whenever a random encounter is needed or when the party needs a social hub. Describe it as a collection of multi-legged huts that skitter away or approach the players on spider-like limbs.
3.  **Hazard Density:** The Fen is not a flat plane; it is full of "pools," "willow copses," and "mire." The ground is unstable, and the "stepping stones" are often traps (the Orogolutz).

**Gameplay tip for the DM:** Treat the Fen as an "atmosphere-first" space. Rather than drawing a map, describe the density of the air, the smell of the peat, and the sounds of the creatures. The map is in the *mist*—distance is measured by the clarity of sight, not by feet or inches.
