Question 4.1 — When governments fall, how does order degrade over months and years, and what brings it back?

Aims and Subset Questions

Assigned question (4.1): In catastrophes, how does law and order break down over the medium to long term, and how is it re-established if the previous government or ruling party falls? Examine Haiti 2010. Assess willingness to resort to violence when survival and resource competition are at stake. Identify factors that do and do not sustain prosocial behavior. Analyze consolidation into groups and post-catastrophe group dynamics.

Subset questions

  1. What distinguishes short-term antisocial spikes (days) from structural insecurity (years)?
  2. When a state literally collapses — buildings, personnel, prisons — who provides order?
  3. Can foreign peacekeeping substitute for domestic legitimacy?
  4. Do emergent mutual-aid groups persist or morph into armed factions?
  5. Under scarcity, when does cooperation flip to parochialism or predation?
  6. What happens when the ruling party survives physically but loses legitimacy?

Limitations

Theoretical

Medium-term order is a political economy problem, not purely a psychology problem. The same population may be prosocial in camp mutual aid and predatory in gang extortion simultaneously — different group identities, different norms.[1] "Law and order" itself is contested: gangs in Haiti function as de facto governance for some residents while terrorizing others.[2]

Methodological

Haiti data is fragmented across NGO reports, UN Security Council documents, and journalism; crime statistics are unreliable where most violence goes unreported.[3] Comparative catastrophe studies overweight natural disasters and underweight epidemic-driven collapse (the Apocalypse Story scenario).[4]

How Law and Order Is Re-established (or Not)

When a government falls or is physically destroyed, three pathways dominate the literature:[5][6]

Pathway A: Victor's army / revolutionary consolidation

The armed group that wins the crisis becomes the state. Rare after natural catastrophe unless catastrophe triggers revolution (e.g., Ming peasant rebellions after famine and plague).[7]

Pathway B: External intervention and hollow state

Foreign peacekeepers and NGOs provide services while nominally supporting a surviving government. Haiti 2010 is the archetype: MINUSTAH (already present since 2004) expanded by 3,500 personnel via UNSCR 1908;[8] U.S. Operation Unified Response deployed 22,000 personnel.[9] Over 90% of aid bypassed Haitian ministries.[10] Result: a "hollowed out" state — elections continued, but real protection came from gangs, UN police, or not at all.[11]

Pathway C: Warlord / gang governance

Non-state armed actors fill the vacuum. Citizens "are more likely to turn to a gangleader, warlord, private security firm or multinational peacekeeping force, than to the security services of their own state."[11] Warlords "shape-shift" and adapt rather than disappear under intervention.[6] In Haiti, baz self-defense groups formed after 2010 were overtaken by younger, more violent gangs by 2012.[12]

Violence Under Resource Competition

When survival goods — food, water, shelter, medicine — are scarce, violence is not automatic but becomes rational for actors who can capture distribution chains.[14]

Scarcity → violence pathways

  • Distribution capture: Armed groups control aid convoys, ports, and markets. G9 held up fuel trucks at Port-au-Prince port (2021).[15]
  • Predation on the vulnerable: Displacement camps: 250+ rapes reported in first 150 days; armed men roaming camps after dark.[3]
  • Prison breach: ~4,000 escaped inmates increased predatory capacity immediately.[11]
  • Survival sex economy: Women traded sex for food; pregnancy rates in camps 3× urban pre-quake norms.[16]
  • Parochialism under loss: Cyclone Pam study: property damage decreased prosocial giving; seeing others' distress increased it.[17]

What Enables and Blocks Prosocial Behavior in Extreme Catastrophe

Factors that sustain prosocial behavior

  • Shared fate / common identity: Emergent social identities form around shared disaster experience, enabling mutual aid even among strangers.[1]
  • Catastrophe compassion: Documented altruism spikes under crisis; belief in others' benevolence facilitates cooperation.[19]
  • Pre-existing community bonds: Social capital and "informal insurance" networks speed recovery.[20]
  • Visible coordination success: Observing others help increases helping behavior (especially for indirectly affected).[1]
  • Faith and grassroots organizations: Churches and community groups in Haiti coordinated shelter and food when state absent.[18]
  • External aid arrival: Burglary spikes in USVI after Irma declined once humanitarian aid flowed — need-based theft stopped.[21]

Factors that block or erode prosocial behavior

  • Perceived unequal aid distribution: Emergent groups dissolve when members feel unfairly treated.[22]
  • Secondary stressors persisting: Ongoing camp conditions, not the quake itself, erode solidarity over 12–18 months.[22]
  • State absence beyond ~6 months: Without prosecution and services, predation becomes normalized.
  • Political manipulation of armed groups: Baz morph from defense to offense when politicians arm them.[12]
  • Material loss without emotional social contact: Property damage alone reduces prosocial allocations.[17]
  • Rumor and scapegoating: Kanto 1923 pattern — false attribution of looting to minorities produced massacre.[23]
  • International bypass of local institutions: Signals state irrelevance; "Build Back Better" without local ownership failed.[13]

Group Consolidation and Post-Catastrophe Dynamics

After catastrophe, individuals rarely remain atomized — they consolidate into groups with distinct functions. Social identity theory predicts these formations; Haiti illustrates the full spectrum from therapeutic community to armed federation.[1]

Group evolution pattern (Haiti model)

Community baz (defense) → armed gang (territory) → political instrument (patron) → autonomous federation (G9) → state challenger (blocks fuel, demands PM resignation)

After Moïse assassination (2021), gangs decoupled from patrons and became "overlords of Haiti."[2] By 2023–24, rival federations merged for offensives.[2]

Group dynamics that shape the landscape

  • Identity shift: From "earthquake survivors" to "camp residents" to "gang territory residents" — each identity carries different norms.[22]
  • Youth recruitment: ~200 armed groups by 2023; structural poverty supplies recruits.[25]
  • Federation logic: Gangs ally for turf wars, then negotiate truces to preserve resources.[2]
  • Gendered violence as group strategy: Sexual violence in camps served to terrorize and control, not merely individual crime.[3]
  • Decline of emergent prosocial groups: Without institutional support, mutual-aid groups fade by 12–18 months unless secondary stressors maintain common fate.[22]

Haiti 2010: Detailed Case Study

Pre-existing fragility (context)

Haiti in January 2010 was not a blank slate. MINUSTAH had occupied since 2004. Armed baz dated to Aristide's 1990s patronage networks.[12] 170,000–220,000 illegal weapons circulated.[11] Cite Soleil was already a "black hole" of insecurity.[11] The earthquake did not create fragility — it annihilated what little state capacity remained.

Literal state collapse (January 2010)

  • ~230,000 dead; 1.5 million displaced; damage ~120% of GDP.[13]
  • 27 of 28 federal buildings destroyed; National Palace in ruins.[11]
  • ~20% of federal employees killed.[11]
  • ~4,000 prisoners escaped.[11]
  • 101 UN personnel killed including mission leadership.[8]

Why "Build Back Better" failed to restore order

Echebarria (2023) identifies six structural failures:[13]

  1. Reconstruction and development objectives combined with insufficient resources
  2. Security built on unsustainable model — weak, underfunded HNP
  3. Divisive politics never addressed as root cause
  4. Deep mistrust between Haitian authorities and donors
  5. State-building failed; institutions deteriorated further
  6. Project-by-project donor approach dispersed effort

The earthquake "did not serve as a catalyst to unite the nation."[13] International community "contributing to the pernicious cycle of sidelining country institutions and eroding state capacity."[13]

Prosocial strands that persisted (and faded)

  • Immediate: Neighbors dug survivors from rubble; Argentine field hospital operated; Haitian diaspora remittances surged.[9]
  • Medium: Camp committees organized; women's groups (KOFAVIK) documented violence.[27]
  • Fade: By 2016, ~62,600 still in camps; solidarity eroded under chronic insecurity, unequal aid, and gang recruitment of youth.[28]

Conclusion

Concise Conclusion

Medium- to long-term law and order after catastrophe follows a different logic than the first 72 hours studied in Q4. Immediate prosocial behavior is real but temporary. Whether order re-establishes depends on whether a legitimate monopoly on force emerges — through state rebuilding, victor consolidation, or sustained external intervention.

Haiti 2010 teaches that catastrophe atop a weak state does not produce renewal; it accelerates fragmentation. The government survived nominally but lost physical capacity, legitimacy, and the monopoly on violence. NGOs and peacekeepers filled service gaps but could not substitute for prosecution, political consensus, or equitable resource distribution. Self-defense groups consolidated into gangs; gangs consolidated into federations; federations became de facto rulers.

Prosocial behavior persists when: common identity is maintained, aid is perceived as fair, the state (or a trusted alternative) provides security, and secondary stressors are resolved.

Violence escalates when: resources are captured by armed groups, impunity is total, political elites arm factions, and international actors bypass local institutions.

For the Apocalypse Story epidemic scenario: A 50–90% population loss over weeks resembles Haiti's institutional annihilation more than Japan's Kobe earthquake. The critical medium-term variables are: who controls food and medicine distribution, whether emergent mutual-aid groups are armed and politicized, and whether any surviving authority can prosecute predation. Without those, prosocial "catastrophe compassion" erodes within 12–18 months — and group consolidation favors whoever offers protection, however violently.
