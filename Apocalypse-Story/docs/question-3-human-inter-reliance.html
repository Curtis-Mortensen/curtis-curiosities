Human Inter-Reliance: Medical Dependency in US Collapse Scenarios

Research Report — Apocalypse Story Project, Question 3. How many people in the United States would likely die without ongoing care, medical attention, or medicine? What do disaster preparedness plans say? What have institutions war-gamed?

Prepared July 2026 · Self-contained research document · General audience

1. Aims of This Document

This report maps how medically interdependent the US population is, and what credible plans and exercises say about mortality when care systems fail — not from a novel pathogen directly, but from the loss of medicines, clinics, electricity, and professional caregivers.

The Apocalypse Story project investigates a fast-moving epidemic with high mortality. Question 3 sits in a distinct lane from psychology (Q2) or law-and-order breakdown (Q4): it asks how many Americans are tethered to medical infrastructure such that its collapse would produce deaths in addition to those from infection.

We pursue four deliverables:

  1. Quantify overlapping populations dependent on prescriptions, devices, facilities, or skilled caregivers.
  2. Review what federal and state preparedness documents say about at-risk and medically fragile populations.
  3. Survey institutional tabletop exercises and functional simulations (HHS, Johns Hopkins, DoD).
  4. Extract lessons from real disasters where care interruption drove excess mortality.

2. Precursor and Subset Questions

Before estimating deaths, we decomposed the main question:

2.1 Precursor questions

  • Definitions: What counts as "medical dependency" — daily essential medicine, electricity-dependent devices, facility-based care, or any chronic condition?
  • Time horizon: Deaths without insulin occur in days; without dialysis in roughly two weeks; without antihypertensives over months. What scenario duration are we modeling?
  • Partial vs. total collapse: Do pharmacies close nationally, or only regionally? Is grid power lost, or only supply chains?
  • Informal care: Can family caregivers substitute for home health, hospice, or nursing staff?
  • Overlap: The same person may be elderly, on insulin, on oxygen, and in a nursing home — how do we avoid double-counting?

3. Limitations

3.1 Theoretical limitations

  • Human adaptation is unpredictable. Neighbors sharing insulin, black-market supply chains, or improvised dialysis cannot be modeled reliably [26].
  • Geography dominates. Rural counties with high DME prevalence and long outage durations face different fates than urban areas with walkable clinics [27].
  • Collapse is not binary. Partial continuity — one functioning hospital, one pharmacy — changes outcomes dramatically; exercises often assume worse-case simultaneous failure [17][18].
  • Overlap and substitution. Official counts (Medicare-only emPOWER data, single-condition registries) undercount total population at risk [1][28].

3.2 Methodological and literature limitations

  • No federal "deaths without care" registry. The US tracks disease mortality and disaster direct deaths, but does not publish a national estimate of fatalities attributable solely to loss of medical infrastructure in total-collapse scenarios [28][29].
  • Administrative claims ≠ clinical acuity. emPOWER identifies beneficiaries with claims for device types, not verified in-home use on a given day [1][2].
  • Exercise outputs are policy tools, not forecasts. Dark Winter's 1–3 million deaths assumed smallpox with no further vaccine; Crimson Contagion's 586,000 assumed unmitigated influenza — neither isolates care-disruption mortality [17][18].
  • Disaster attribution is contested. Puerto Rico excess-mortality estimates range from ~1,200 to ~4,645 to 2,975 (official revision), with debate over baseline years and survivor bias [21][30].
  • Strength of evidence varies. Power-outage → hospitalization links are increasingly robust (case-crossover designs, nursing-home cohorts) [24][31][32]. National collapse modeling remains sparse and scenario-dependent.

3.3 Field maturity

Medical surge planning matured after 2001 (anthrax, Dark Winter) and 2005 (Hurricane Katrina, Atlantic Storm). The HHS emPOWER program (2014–present) represents the strongest population mapping tool. However, quantitative national modeling of care-disruption mortality in total societal collapse remains an gap — planners focus on weeks-long disasters with federal aid, not months without resupply [1][14][33].

6. What Disaster Preparedness Plans State

6.1 National Health Security Strategy (NHSS)

The NHSS (2019–2022) requires integrated public–private medical surge, regional coordination, and explicit attention to at-risk individuals — defined as those needing extra help with communication, medical care, independence, supervision, or transportation before, during, and after incidents [14][15]. Statutory requirements (42 U.S.C. §300hh-1) mandate plans for hospital surge, alternate care sites, volunteer credentialing, and altered standards of care when resources are exhausted [46].

What it says about care-dependent mortality: Indirectly. NHSS emphasizes maintaining continuity of care and expanding surge rather than forecasting how many die if care stops entirely. It assumes federal and regional assets can augment local failure [14][33].

6.2 ASPR / HHS emPOWER and DME guidance

ASPR TRACIE guidance states plainly that prolonged power outages create "life-threatening situations" for electricity-dependent DME users, who may overwhelm EMS and hospitals or shelter-in-place without resources [16]. The emPOWER program (ASPR + CMS, expanded 2014) maps Medicare beneficiaries using 14 device categories plus outpatient dialysis, home oxygen, hospice, and home health — updated monthly to ZIP code level for public-health authorities [1][2][47].

6.3 GAO medical surge findings (2008)

GAO found states planning for four surge components — extra beds, alternate care sites, volunteer registries, and crisis standards of care — but lacking shared national guidance for allocating scarce resources (ventilators, ICU beds) [33]. Crisis standards of care (CSC) plans determine who receives care when demand exceeds supply; implementation varies widely by state [18][48].

6.4 FEMA functional-needs and shelter integration

FEMA requires general-population shelters to accommodate individuals with functional needs rather than segregating them. Planning guides (2007, 2010) stress power backup for medical devices and integration of disability/access services [38][49]. FEMA household surveys show only ~52% have disaster supplies; 65% had no adequate disaster plan (Petkova et al., cited in NASEM nursing disaster chapter) [50].

6.5 Pandemic Influenza and Biological Incident Annexes

HHS pandemic plans (Biological Incident Annex, Pandemic Crisis Action Plan) prioritize medical countermeasure distribution and hospital surge. Crimson Contagion (2019) tested these and found them insufficient for supply-chain and funding gaps [18][51]. Plans assume pharmaceutical and device supply continues at some level — not total cessation.

6.6 Department of Defense

USNORTHCOM's Pandemic & Infectious Disease Response Plan (2021) focuses on force protection, beneficiary care via MHS, and Defense Support of Civil Authorities when requested. It incorporates lessons from H1N1, Ebola, and COVID-19 but does not publish civilian care-disruption mortality estimates [20][52]. DoD's role in civilian medically fragile populations is supportive (ESF #8 coordination), not primary [53].

7.1 Crimson Contagion detail (most operationally realistic)

The 70-page After-Action Report (FOIA release, 2020) involved 19 federal agencies, 12 states, 74 local health departments, and 87 hospitals [18][57]. Findings directly relevant to medical dependency:

  • Insufficient federal funding for severe pandemic response [18]
  • US lacks domestic manufacturing for PPE, needles, syringes, N95s, and most countermeasures [18][51]
  • States reported "varying degrees" of crisis-standards-of-care maturity — from none to partial ventilator-allocation protocols [18][48]
  • Resource-request processes between states and federal government were non-standardized [18]
  • Scenario parameters at day 47: 110M illnesses, 7.7M hospitalizations, 586,000 deaths — dominated by direct infection, but hospital saturation implies deferred care for non-COVID-like conditions [18][58]

9. Synthesis: How Many Would Likely Die?

Bottom line: No US government agency publishes a credible single number for "Americans who would die if all medical care and supply chains stopped." What exists is a layered picture: identifiable high-acuity populations (~3–6 million minimally, with overlap), broader elevated-risk groups (~10–20 million within months), and a very large chronic-disease population whose mortality would accelerate over a year.

9.1 Scenario A — Total nationwide cessation (worst case, months)

If pharmacies, hospitals, electricity, and supply chains fail concurrently across the US for months (beyond any modeled federal exercise):

  • Near-certain high mortality (~500k–1M+ in first 2–4 weeks, rough order-of-magnitude): In-center dialysis (~433k), insulin-dependent (~2–8M depending on definition), ventilator/BiPAP-dependent (subset of emPOWER), and a large fraction of ~1.24M nursing-home residents. Not all would die (some overlap; some informal care), but historical disaster data show frail and device-dependent populations suffer disproportionately [8][3][21][24].
  • Substantial additional mortality over 1–6 months (~1–3M+ rough OOM): Transplant recipients, active cancer treatment, HIV without ART, home health dependents, psychiatric maintenance failures, and cardiovascular patients off medications [5][43][9][45].
  • Accelerated mortality over 6–24 months: Much of the ~194M chronic-disease population would experience worsening outcomes; magnitude depends on age structure and informal support — essentially unmodeled at national scale [12].

These ranges are author synthesis, not official estimates. They extrapolate from population counts and disaster proportionalities (e.g., Maria: ~⅓ of excess deaths from care disruption in a partial island-wide failure) [21].

9.2 Scenario B — Pandemic surge (Crimson Contagion-class)

Exercises model 586,000 direct pandemic deaths at day 47 with 7.7M hospitalizations — before counting care deferred for unrelated conditions [18]. COVID-19 real-world data showed millions of delayed-care encounters and sustained non-COVID excess mortality [25][62]. Care-disruption deaths in this scenario are likely hundreds of thousands over 12–24 months even if the pathogen's direct CFR is contained — but separating direct vs. indirect remains methodologically hard [63].

9.3 Scenario C — Regional grid failure (empirically grounded)

Regional disasters produce measurable excess death fractions from care disruption alone:

  • Puerto Rico: ~⅓ of excess deaths (1,500+ of ~4,645 in survey window) [21]
  • Katrina chronic-care survivors: 20.6% interrupted treatment [22]
  • Nursing-home power loss: statistically significant mortality increases [24][60]

Scaling regional partial failure to national total failure is not linear — but these fractions validate that care disruption is a first-order killer, not a rounding error.

10. Conclusion

The United States has built sophisticated mapping tools (emPOWER) and surge planning frameworks (NHSS, CSC), but neither federal preparedness documents nor major exercises produce a single authoritative estimate of deaths from total loss of medical care. The evidence converges on three conclusions:

  1. Medical dependency is massive and growing. Tens of millions rely on daily prescriptions; millions on electricity, facilities, or skilled caregivers; overlap is substantial [2][12][28].
  2. Care disruption kills reliably in real disasters. From Maria to Irma to COVID care avoidance, interrupted treatment is repeatedly implicated in one-fifth to one-third of excess deaths in studied events [21][22][25].
  3. Institutional exercises confirm system fragility but model infection deaths, not care-collapse deaths. Dark Winter, Crimson Contagion, and Clade X stress zero surge capacity and resource rationing — implying dependent patients would be among the first rationed out — but do not isolate that mortality stream [17][18][19].

For Apocalypse Story scenario work, a defensible planning assumption is that among survivors of the initial epidemic wave, an additional 5–15% of the pre-crisis medically dependent population could die within 6 months from care and supply failure, with the exact figure highly sensitive to whether electricity, local pharmacies, and informal networks persist. This bracket is synthesis, not prophecy.

11. Further Research Directions

  • Commission or locate agent-based models that separate direct infection mortality from care-disruption mortality with explicit insulin/dialysis/DME modules.
  • Merge emPOWER ZIP data with grid-outage probability and pharmacy density maps for geospatial risk scoring [27].
  • Systematic review of crisis-standards-of-care implementation by state (post-Crimson Contagion).
  • Interview ASPR/regional ESF-8 coordinators on assumed timelines for total pharmaceutical resupply failure.
  • Quantify insulin stockpile duration at household and pharmacy level nationally.
  • Explore Q7 follow-up: epidemic-specific connectivity (internet, fuel, water) failure rates and interaction with medical dependency.
