Overview

Forecasting a subscription business means projecting how customers move through states — trial, active, upgraded, downgraded, churned, win-back — and translating those movements into recurring revenue, cash, and operating costs. The model you choose depends on whether you are exploring futures (scenario planning) or measuring the past (analytics from billing events).

This document covers three complementary approaches:

  1. Custom forecasting script — a lightweight cohort state machine you design and own end-to-end.
  2. Theseus Growth — MIT-licensed Python library for retention curves and forward user projections.
  3. Tidemill — open-source subscription analytics engine that computes MRR, churn, retention, and LTV from billing data.
What this document is not It does not ship runnable forecasting software. It defines the math, data shapes, and architectural seams so you can evaluate build vs. adopt before writing code.

Revenue waterfall (MRR movement)

Regardless of pricing shape, subscription revenue forecasting usually decomposes monthly recurring revenue (MRR) into a waterfall. This is the lingua franca of SaaS finance: 

MRR(t) = MRR(t-1)
       + New_MRR(t)          ← brand-new customers
       + Expansion_MRR(t)    ← upgrades, seat adds, usage growth
       - Contraction_MRR(t)  ← downgrades, seat removals
       - Churned_MRR(t)      ← lost revenue from cancellations
       + Reactivation_MRR(t) ← win-back of previously churned customers

Net Revenue Retention (NRR) captures expansion minus churn on the existing base: NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion − Contraction − Churn) / Starting MRR. NRR above 100% means the existing customer base grows even with zero new logos.

Costs & expenditures

Variable (scale with customers)

Payment processing (~2–3%), hosting per active user, support tickets, referral credits, win-back discounts, sales commissions on new ARR.

Semi-variable (step functions)

Customer success headcount bands, infra tiers, marketing spend tied to acquisition targets.

Fixed (time-based)

Salaries, office, core platform R&D, legal. Often modeled as monthly burn independent of subscriber count in early-stage forecasts.

A complete forecast links the subscriber model to a simple P&L: Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS, Operating Income = Gross Profit − OpEx, Cash = prior Cash + Collections − Expenses − CAC spend.

Retention

Cohort retention is the most honest way to model subscription decay. You group customers by signup month and measure what fraction remain active at month 1, 2, 3, … Retention typically follows a decay curve (log, power, or Weibull fit) rather than a flat monthly churn rate — though flat churn is a common simplifying assumption.

Gross Revenue Retention (GRR) excludes expansion; it answers “did we keep the dollars we had last year?” NRR includes expansion and is the headline metric for B2B SaaS health (median benchmarks often land around 90% GRR and 100–110% NRR depending on segment).

Viral growth

Viral growth is not magic — it is a second acquisition channel with near-zero marginal CAC. Model it explicitly rather than folding it into “organic new signups”: 

Viral_new(t) = Active(t) × invites_per_active × invite_conversion_rate
             = Active(t) × K   (when measured per cycle)

Total_new(t) = Paid_new(t) + Viral_new(t) + Reactivated(t)

K usually decays as markets saturate. Scenario planning should include a “viral fade” assumption (e.g. K × (1 − penetration)) rather than assuming K > 1 forever.

Churn & win-back (reactivation)

Churned customers are not gone — they are in a latent pool. Industry data suggests a meaningful share of “new” subscriptions are actually returning customers (Recurly’s 2026 benchmark: roughly 1 in 4 new sign-ups from returning subscribers across their merchant base). Reactivation CAC is typically far below cold acquisition because you already have email, product familiarity, and often saved payment methods.

Model win-back as a separate flow from the churned pool:

Churned_pool(t) = Churned_pool(t-1) + Churned(t) - Reactivated(t)

Reactivated(t) = Churned_pool(t-1) × reactivation_rate(t)
               × eligible_fraction

Segment reactivation by cancel reason (price, missing feature, temporary need, payment failure) because each segment has different rates and incentive costs. A win-back campaign only makes economic sense when expected_LTV × reactivation_rate > outreach_cost + discount_cost.

Cohort matrix representation

The workhorse data structure is a cohort retention matrix: rows are signup cohorts, columns are months since signup, cells are retained subscribers (or retained revenue). Forward projection fills future columns using a retention curve, then sums across cohorts for total actives and MRR. 

         M0    M1    M2    M3   ...
Cohort A  100   85    72    65
Cohort B   50   44    39    —
Cohort C  120  102    —     —
─────────────────────────────────
Total     270  231   111    65  →  sum columns for active count each month

Scenario Dimensions

A useful forecast exposes levers you can sweep for sensitivity analysis. Below are the dimensions most relevant to subscription retention, viral growth, churn, and win-back.

Scenario leverLow caseBase caseHigh case
Monthly logo churn 8% (consumer) 4% 2% (enterprise)
NRR 85% 100% 120%
Viral K 0.1 0.35 0.7+ (rare sustained)
Reactivation rate (monthly, eligible pool) 1% 3% 8% (strong win-back program)
Paid CAC Rising with competition Flat Falling with brand / SEO
Price change −10% (promo) Flat +15% (value add)
Worked example (illustrative numbers only) Suppose 1,000 actives at $30 ARPU ($30k MRR), 4% monthly churn, 50 paid new/month at $200 CAC, K = 0.3 (30 viral signups/month), and 2% monthly reactivation from a 2,000-person churned pool (40 win-backs). Net subscriber change ≈ 50 + 30 + 40 − 40 = +80/month before expansion. MRR grows if ARPU holds and expansion offsets any downgrades. This is the level of arithmetic a custom script should make transparent and editable.

Roll-Your-Own Forecasting Script

Building your own script trades integration work for total control. The architecture below is intentionally barebones — thin behavior, clear files — matching an MVP that can grow without rewriting.

Recommended architecture

  ┌─────────────┐    ┌──────────────┐    ┌─────────────────┐
  │  inputs/    │    │   engine/    │    │   outputs/      │
  │  YAML or    │───►│  monthly     │───►│  CSV / JSON /   │
  │  CSV params │    │  simulation  │    │  HTML report    │
  └─────────────┘    └──────┬───────┘    └─────────────────┘
                            │
         ┌──────────────────┼──────────────────┐
         ▼                  ▼                  ▼
   retention.py      acquisition.py     revenue.py
   (cohort curves)   (paid + viral K)     (MRR waterfall)
         │                  │                  │
         └──────── churn.py ─┴── reactivation.py ┘
                            │
                       costs.py → pnl.py

Suggested module responsibilities

ModuleResponsibilityKey outputs
models/assumptions.py Typed dataclass or dict schema for all scenario levers (churn, K, prices, OpEx) Validated config object
models/cohort.py Cohort matrix: new signups per period, retention curve application Active users by month
engine/acquisition.py Paid new from budget/CAC; viral new from K × actives; optional caps New cohort sizes per month
engine/churn.py Apply retention or churn rate; route exits to churned pool Churned count, churned pool size
engine/reactivation.py Win-back from churned pool by segment and rate Reactivated count (feeds acquisition)
engine/revenue.py Plan mix, ARPU, expansion/contraction; MRR waterfall MRR, ARR, NRR components
engine/costs.py COGS %, fixed OpEx, CAC spend, win-back incentives Expense lines
run_forecast.py Loop months 1..N; call modules in order; append rows Time series DataFrame
report.py (optional) Emit charts/tables — or export CSV for Sheets/Excel Human-readable scenario comparison

Monthly simulation loop (pseudocode)

for month in range(1, horizon + 1):
    paid_new   = acquisition.paid(month, assumptions)
    viral_new  = acquisition.viral(active_prev, assumptions.K)
    reactivated = reactivation.from_pool(churned_pool, assumptions)

    new_total  = paid_new + viral_new + reactivated
    cohorts.add(month, new_total)

    active     = cohorts.sum_retained(month, retention_curve)
    churned    = active_prev + new_total - active - reactivated  # reconciliation
    churned_pool += churned - reactivated

    mrr        = revenue.mrr(active, plan_mix, assumptions)
    expenses   = costs.compute(mrr, new_total, paid_new, reactivated, assumptions)

    record(month, active, mrr, expenses, churned_pool, ...)

Strengths & weaknesses

Strengths

  • Exact fit to your pricing quirks and win-back logic
  • Full transparency — every formula is yours to audit
  • No vendor lock-in; runs in a notebook or CI
  • Easy to add scenario sweeps (Monte Carlo later if needed)

Weaknesses

  • You maintain billing-ingest and metric definitions yourself
  • Retention curve fitting is easy to get wrong without validation
  • No built-in Stripe/Lago connector — you build ETL
  • Reporting and dashboards are extra work
Best when You are in early-stage scenario planning, have non-standard pricing, or need win-back and viral levers modeled exactly how you think about the business — and you are willing to invest a few hundred lines of Python rather than adopt a framework.

Theseus Growth (Open Source Library)

Repository: github.com/egecakiroglu/theseus_growth · License: MIT · Install: pip install theseus_growth

Theseus is a Python library created by Eric Benjamin Seufert (Heracles / Mobile Dev Memo) for cohort analysis and growth projections. It is explicitly designed for marketing budget planning, scenario analysis, and forward user projections — not full P&L or billing ingest.

What it does well

  • Retention profiles — fit log, exp, linear, quad, Weibull, power, or interpolate curves to sparse retention data points.
  • Forward DAU/MAU projection — project_cohorted_DAU combines new-user cohorts with a retention profile to project active users over time.
  • Age segmentation — project_aged_DAU splits users by tenure (useful for monetization timing).
  • Export — to_excel and to_json for downstream reporting.

Architecture (library-centric)

  Your notebook / script
         │
         ▼
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  theseus_growth                      │
  │  ├── create_profile(days, retention) │──► best-fit curve + projection
  │  ├── project_cohorted_DAU(...)       │──► pandas DataFrame of actives
  │  ├── project_aged_DAU(...)           │──► tenure-segmented actives
  │  └── plot_retention / to_excel       │
  └──────────────────────────────────────┘
         │
         ▼
  You multiply actives × ARPU, add viral cohorts externally,
  layer churned-pool win-back in your own wrapper script

Mapping to your concerns

ConcernTheseus supportGap you fill manually
Subscription retention Core strength — retention profiles and cohort projections Map DAU/MAU to paying subscribers if not 1:1
Viral growth Not built-in — you supply cohort sizes (can compute K × actives yourself) Viral loop and K decay model
Churn Implicit via retention curve (churn = 1 − retention) Explicit churned pool accounting
Win-back Not built-in Add reactivation cohorts to the cohorts input list
Revenue & costs Not included — user/projections only ARPU, MRR waterfall, OpEx in wrapper

Typical usage pattern

import theseus_growth as th

profile = th.create_profile(days=[1,7,30,90], retention_values=[80,55,30,12])
cohorts = [100, 120, 150, ...]  # new users per period; include win-backs as positive entries
dau_df  = th.project_cohorted_DAU(profile, periods=36, cohorts=cohorts, start_date=1)

# Your layer:
mrr = dau_df.sum(axis=0) * arpu_assumption
Best when You want statistically sensible retention curve fitting and cohort-based user projections without building scipy curve-fit plumbing — and you are comfortable wrapping the library with your own revenue, viral, and win-back logic (essentially Approach ① with Theseus as the retention engine).

Tidemill (Open Source Analytics Engine)

Repository: github.com/ondraz/tidemill · Python · FastAPI · PostgreSQL · Stripe / Lago / Kill Bill integrations

Tidemill is an open-source subscription analytics engine — transparent, SQL-backed metric computation from billing events. It answers “what happened?” with auditable MRR, churn, retention, LTV, and trial metrics. Forward forecasting is not its primary job; you would use its historical outputs as calibrated inputs to a forecaster (Approach ① or ②).

Deployment profile

Tidemill is heavier than a notebook script: PostgreSQL, optional Kafka for webhook ingestion, FastAPI service. The payoff is a single auditable source of truth for subscription metrics that finance and product can agree on — reducing the “two spreadsheets don’t match” problem before you forecast forward.

Best when You already have (or will have) Stripe or an open-source billing system and need trustworthy historical MRR/churn/retention before layering scenario models. Pair Tidemill (backward-looking) with a custom or Theseus-based forecaster (forward-looking).

Decision Guide

Build custom (①)
Pre-product or spreadsheet replacement; non-standard pricing; win-back and viral logic are central to your narrative; no billing volume yet.
Add Theseus (②)
You have sparse retention benchmarks and want credible cohort projections without writing curve-fit code; revenue layer stays yours.
Deploy Tidemill (③)
Stripe-backed subscriptions are live; leadership needs trusted MRR/churn/retention before trusting any forward model.

Other open-source options (honorable mentions)

Not one of the three primary approaches, but worth knowing during research:

  • Burnless — self-hosted FP&A with dependency-graph forecasting, cap table, full P&L (broader than subscription cohorts).
  • lucius-ltv — Bayesian sBG model for LTV projection from cohort matrices (PyMC).
  • retention-cohort-analyzer — lightweight cohort tables and LTV from event data.
  • ChurnGuard AI — ML churn prediction (who will leave), not forward revenue forecasting.

Suggested research next steps

  1. Write down your pricing structure and which subscriber states matter (trial? seats? usage?).
  2. List data you already have vs. assumptions you will guess (retention curve, K, win-back rate).
  3. If billing is live, prototype Tidemill against a Stripe test export to see metric reconciliation.
  4. Sketch a 12-month scenario in a spreadsheet using the growth accounting formula; that becomes the spec for Approach ①.
  5. Decide whether viral and win-back are first-class model flows or sensitivity-only levers.
