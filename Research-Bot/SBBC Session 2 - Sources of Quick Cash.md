## Serving Existing Customers with Empathy

The first place we recommend starting is with your pre-existing clients—the customers you have spent years, time, energy, and resources acquiring. Reaching out to them right now is important, but we encourage our clients to treat their customers more like neighbors and less like customers. In your social media posts, outreach emails, and marketing messages across different channels, think about your customers as neighbors. They are not necessarily in buying mode or that buyer psychology. A little empathy and care will go a long way because that is probably what they need right now more than your goods and services.  
We have seen examples of ownership or leadership sending email blasts that simply check in on people. Rather than focusing on what the organization is doing internally, they focus on the customer and make sure the customer is doing okay. When reaching out to pre-existing customers, let them know your employees are okay, that you are taking care of them, and that you are doing your best to look after them. Treat your store, whether you have a brick-and-mortar location or deliver your services or products another way, as part of the community. Refer to your business as part of the community, rather than as a separate brand or entity.  
Then there are reviews. People are more frustrated than ever because many of us cannot follow our regular routines or visit the stores we like and buy the products we want. We have seen a large increase in negative reviews across every industry. It is not necessarily about the business; people are frustrated with the situation and are taking it out wherever they can. The most vocal detractors are often reviewers. It is important to respond with empathy, appreciation for their continued support, solutions when they ask for them, and, overall, thoughtfulness and patience.  
I included an amazing example on the right side of the slide. A frustrated customer had visited a store where it did not seem that employees were using face masks. The organization's CEO responded by explaining in detail all of the procedures they had implemented. The CEO explained that employees could choose whether to wear a face mask because some felt they were touching their faces too often to adjust their masks, which had become another concern about spreading the virus. I thought this was a great example.  
When people reach out, address their concerns. We know there is a tremendous amount of fear and frustration, so acknowledge as quickly as possible that what they are feeling is real. Then explain what you are doing to address their situation or concerns within the constraints the virus has imposed on all of us.  
Circling back to the bottom of that review, you will notice that we adjusted the actual deliverables for this client. They now offer curbside pickup and free delivery, which they had not offered before. Giving customers an alternative is extremely important because it shows that you are going the extra step. There are a few more items on the list. When speaking about essentials, be aware that we have seen a lot of tone-deaf messaging, mostly through social media but also through other marketing channels. Businesses are still trying to let people know they are open and should come by and buy things, when people are not very receptive to businesses trying to sell to them right now.  
If you offer essential services or products and are an essential business, focus your messaging on those essentials and move away from your typical cadence centered on the products and services you want to sell. Stick to the basics. If you do not have many essentials or are not an essential business, simply let customers know what you do and what you do best in a passive, empathetic way. You might share your store hours or say that you are ready to open and explain how you will take care of customers when you do.  
Another easy way to reach customers is by sharing community efforts. Many people have started small movements to raise money for those who have been affected. If you can donate gift cards to those efforts or find ways to volunteer and support them, share that on social media. It lets people know that you are part of their community and are not always trying to sell something.  
We were working with a realty group in Flagstaff. We do not necessarily recommend trying to predict the future or going out on a limb, but this is a good time to publish information relevant to your industry, explain how the virus has affected it, and offer some perspective on what the future may hold. A lot of people are fearful and reserved, and they are not necessarily picturing what life will be like when we come out of this. If you can paint that picture and place your industry, service, or product within it, you can get a little ahead of them with your branding efforts.  
One example on the slide involved pulling an ad. KFC had been running its finger-licking-good ad, which felt insensitive, so the company pulled it. That illustrates what we are discussing: we have shifted many strategies, pulling ads that felt insensitive and replacing them with messages that address the points Rand mentioned. It is important to reflect on what you are already putting out and determine whether it is appropriate for the moment. Adidas also sent an email saying it would keep its stores open and practice social distancing. The backlash quickly got out of control, and the company had to follow up with a statement that its employees were more important than its bottom line. Be sensitive to those issues.

## Finding New Customers and Adapting Offers

Now we come to some of our favorite digital marketing topics. Assuming you have already messaged your existing customer base, how do you acquire new customers and find ways to adjust to the situation? Mike, I will let you take over. We have had to shift many of our clients' strategies. To do that, we have had to think creatively about what they can offer, especially when they are not considered essential businesses.  
For essential retailers, focus on pickup and delivery. People do not want to leave their homes, and we are seeing a lot of curbside pickup, where customers pop their trunks and employees place the purchases inside. People appreciate that low-contact shopping. Office equipment is another booming retail category, including not just furniture but accessories such as webcams, desk accessories, mouse pads, and keyboards—anything people need to work from home. Food delivery and essential items are also important. We have seen restaurants include rolls of toilet paper in takeout bags, which strikes the right note with the community. Some people are ordering just to get the toilet paper.  
People are also spending more time on hobbies while furloughed or at home with their families. We have found that businesses selling hobby kits, puzzles, coloring books, and DIY kits have done well. If you can offer those products, people are looking for them right now.  
For professionals, the opportunity is fairly obvious given that we are on a Zoom call right now. Streaming webinars, web training, and web conferences have exploded online. I have seen people I previously thought were very shy conduct wildly successful web conferences and sign up new clients. One running coach we work with told me that they have signed a new client every week while working 100 percent remotely, which is inspiring. That can be repeated not just by personal trainers, but also by lawyers and consultants.  
Returning to hobby products, some of our retail clients, both e-commerce and brick-and-mortar businesses, have started packaging items together. That is another way to increase your average revenue per ticket: assemble four or five items for someone to use. If you offer things for people to cook, do, or use to teach their children, bundle four or five related items, set a few price points, and sell each bundle as a package.  
We are working with a hardware store in Flagstaff. As the picture shows, selling gardening products is one thing; selling a raised-bed garden package is another. What would typically be a $30, $40, or $50 delivery now averages more than $100 per delivery, so consider packages. In a later slide, we will discuss video. We have produced videos demonstrating DIY packages, and they have performed very well on social media.

## Digital Marketing and Brand Visibility

When you move into digital marketing, this is a great time to promote your brand, especially if you are tuned in to what is happening in people's lives and lead with empathy. We have seen success, both recently and over a long period, with selfie videos that have low production value but a lot of personality. Founders, managers, and employees hold up a phone, walk through their daily lives, and talk to their Facebook followers. That has a high impact in the local community. Here in Flagstaff, people walk into restaurants and businesses and say, I saw you on Facebook. That is exactly the local connection we like to foster. It builds the brand and leads people to choose a local business over the national retailer down the road, once and probably many more times.  
Here are some quick marketing basics. We tell our clients that about half the time they should focus on conversion: they want someone to click, call, or take another action. The other half of the time, they should focus on branding by putting their vision, mission, logo, colors, and fonts into the world and helping people understand who they are and why they do what they do. If customers cannot convert right now, this is a fantastic time to build the brand and reinforce the message behind it.  
Branding is especially important as a touch point for products and services with longer sales cycles, although it matters for shorter sales cycles as well. When discussing a six-month sales cycle in which someone must be approached multiple times, branding ads can create touch points between emails, conferences, seminars, and sales calls. They also give you a chance to communicate with empathy and share your mission and values during a pandemic.  
It is also a good time to get a deal on Facebook and other social media ads. Many competitors and industries are pulling back their spending. I understand why that might make sense for a closed business with a short sales cycle, but we are not recommending it. We are shifting strategies, not ad budgets, and shifting messaging, not ad budgets. We are receiving a much higher return on those ads for branding and views. The $200 we spent last month has gone twice as far this month, and online engagement has also gone twice as far. We are seeing a viral effect that we do not normally see because people are spending more time on social media and sharing more.  
The graphic on the right shows how people in each age group are spending their time during quarantine. At the top is searching for coronavirus and COVID-19 updates, which is fairly obvious. Immediately below that are listening to music, watching movies and shows, watching funny videos, playing mobile games, and looking at memes. Social media captures all of those activities, so that is where people are spending a great deal of time. Being on those platforms means meeting people where they are.  
That is a good point, Mike. People are in entertainment mode. They are not necessarily logging on to shop for a product. They do log on to learn new things, but they also want to be entertained. If you can curate content that is more entertaining and less sales-focused, it will go a long way. When we build shareable social content, we try to hit the three E's: educational, emotional, and entertaining. The order is up to you, but content that delivers those three elements is more likely to be liked and shared.
