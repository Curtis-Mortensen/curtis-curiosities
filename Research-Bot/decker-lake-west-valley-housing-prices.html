1. What This Document Is

This report answers two linked housing questions for a Salt Lake City–area apartment search:

  1. Priority 1 — Proximity: What are the cheapest whole apartments (not rooms) as close as possible to Decker Lake Park in West Valley City, Utah — including studios, 1-bedrooms, 2-bedrooms, duplexes, multiplexes, and split homes?
  2. Priority 2 — Radius trade-off: How do those near-park prices compare with whole apartments within about a 30-minute drive of the park? Does a larger radius unlock meaningfully better deals, and what are the cheapest rates that show up?

It is written for a general audience. You do not need prior knowledge of this repository. Advertised rents change daily; every dollar figure here is a snapshot of asking rent from public listings and market reports as of mid-July 2026, not a locked lease quote.[1]

Bottom line up front: The closest dedicated communities are Apartments at Decker Lake (1BR roughly $1,062–$1,147 list; promotional effective rent sometimes near $993) and the newer Decker Station Apartments (studios from about $1,075, 1BR from about $1,149–$1,295).[1][2][3] The cheapest whole units within a few minutes of the park are older 2-bedroom multiplex / 4-plex listings on Parkway Boulevard and Lake Park Drive in the $835–$925 band — but several showed “check back for availability,” so they are scarce and turn over fast.[4][5][6] Expanding to ~15–30 minutes does find cheaper floors: Magna studios/small 1BRs around $800–$850, and The Redwood (Redwood Rd / Taylorsville edge) studios from $849. Tooele studios from about $650–$799 are cheaper still but sit around ~37 minutes — outside a strict 30-minute ring under typical conditions.[7][8][9][10]

2. Limitations of This Research

2.1 Theoretical / market limitations

Local rental markets are thin, fast-moving inventories. A $835 2-bedroom that appears on Apartments.com today may be leased, bait-priced, or mis-categorized tomorrow. Asking rent is not effective rent: concessions (one month free, eight weeks free), admin fees, CAM fees, utility packages, and income restrictions change the true monthly cost.[2][11] Human search behavior also biases results — people overweight the first community they tour and underweight duplex inventory that does not look like a “complex.”

2.2 Methodology limitations

  • This is a web-listing and market-report snapshot (Apartments.com, Rent.com, Rentler, KSL Homes, property sites, Rentometer, RentCafe, HUD FMR), not a complete census of every landlord in Salt Lake County.
  • Several apartments.com detail pages returned “Check Back Soon for Upcoming Availability” even while still showing a price — treated here as recently listed / availability uncertain.[4][5]
  • Direct curl scrapes of apartments.com were often blocked; pricing was recovered via search-engine snippets, WebFetch markdown extracts, and property-manager pages. Cross-source ranges sometimes disagree by $50–$150 on the same building.[3]
  • Drive times use published city-to-city estimates (MapSof, Travelmath, Uber route pages), not live Google Maps rush-hour routing from the park gate. Peak I-215 / SR-201 traffic can push a “20-minute” trip over 30 minutes.[10][12]
  • Rooms for rent were excluded by design. Aggregators sometimes mis-tag whole 4-plex units as “rooms”; listing text was checked for full-unit language (own kitchen, deposit, lease initiation).[6]
  • Income-restricted / LIHTC properties (e.g., Coppertree Magna, Tuscany Cove) are noted separately — low sticker price only helps if the household qualifies.[13][14]

2.3 Strength of evidence in this field

Applied rental housing research sits between government statistics (ACS, HUD Fair Market Rents), commercial listing platforms, and private rent indexes (Zillow Observed Rent Index, Apartment List, CoStar, RealPage). Official FMRs are strong for program payment standards at the metro level and weaker as street-level shopping guides.[11][15] Listing sites are strong for what is advertised this week and weak for bias (vacancy advertising, bait units, incomplete fee disclosure). The evidence quality here is therefore: good for ranking neighborhoods and spotting cheap floors; fair for exact unit availability; poor for predicting next month’s rents.

4. History of the Fields That Try to Answer “What Does Rent Cost Here?”

Modern local rent comparison sits at the intersection of three research traditions.

4.1 Government rent benchmarks (HUD Fair Market Rents)

Congress created the Section 8 program in 1974; HUD published the first Fair Market Rent (FMR) schedules in 1975 from updated 1970 census data.[16] Early FMRs used the American Housing Survey and a mid-distribution (50th percentile) of recent-mover rents for units meeting quality standards. Methodology later shifted to American Community Survey (ACS) bases, inflation adjustments, and — after heated debate about lagging rents during the COVID spike — incorporation of private rent data alongside CPI starting in FY 2023.[16][17] Today FMRs are generally defined as estimates of the 40th percentile of gross rents (rent + utilities, excluding phone) for standard-quality units in a metro or nonmetro county.[15] For FY 2026, the Salt Lake City HUD Metro FMR Area posts roughly $1,259 studio / $1,456 1BR / $1,747 2BR — useful as a metro ceiling for voucher math, not as Decker Lake street pricing.[18]

4.2 Hedonic and commercial rent indexes

Academic housing economists use hedonic models (controlling for bedrooms, quality, and location) to estimate pure rent inflation. Commercial firms (Zillow, Apartment List, CoStar, RealPage, Moody’s REIS) publish city-level indexes that HUD now partially blends into FMR inflation factors.[17] Consumer portals (Apartments.com, Rent.com, Rentometer, RentCafe) publish city averages from their own listing samples — convenient but sample-dependent, which is why West Valley City “average 1BR” can read ~$1,168 on one site and ~$1,249 on another in the same season.[19][20]

4.3 Consumer apartment search practice

Post-2000 listing aggregators turned rent shopping into a map-filter problem. Utah’s local platforms (Rentler, KSL Classifieds Homes) still matter for small landlords and multiplexes that never appear on national “community” pages.[21] The practical research method used here — proximity rings + whole-unit filter + market averages + fee caveats — is the everyday applied version of that mixed evidence base.

7.2 Just outside 30 minutes — Tooele (for context)

Tooele regularly shows the Salt Lake west-side’s lowest large-community studio floors: The Kirk on Vine studios from $799, 1BR ~$999, 2BR ~$1,099–$1,199; other Tooele studios have listed near $650 with utility fees on top.[9][41] Drive time Tooele ↔ West Valley City is about 37 minutes / 28.5 miles under normal conditions — so it fails a strict “within 30 minutes of Decker Lake” filter, but it answers the broader “are there better deals if I expand the radius?” question with a clear yes for absolute cheapest rates.[10]

9.1 Direct answers to the user’s two parts

  1. Closest + cheap: Hunt whole 2BR units on Parkway Blvd / Lake Park Dr first ($835–$925 / $885). If those are gone, Apartments at Decker Lake 1BR with concessions (~$993–$1,147 list) is the closest dependable community deal; Decker Station is closer in branding but usually costlier for the same bedrooms.
  2. 30-minute radius: Yes — a larger radius finds better deals for studios and small 1-bedrooms, mainly in Magna and along the Redwood Rd corridor (The Redwood). For 2-bedrooms, expanding helps less unless you accept older multiplex stock around $1,175–$1,395 in Magna or catch another near-park outlier. The absolute cheapest rates (~$650–$799 studios) sit in Tooele, typically just beyond 30 minutes.
Practical next step: Same-day call / apply on any Parkway or Lake Park whole unit under $950; same week tour Magna + The Redwood for studios/1BRs; only then lock a Decker Lake complex if proximity outweighs $100–$250/month.

11. Concise Conclusion

For housing as close as possible to Decker Lake Park, the cheapest whole apartments in this snapshot are older multiplex 2-bedrooms on Parkway Boulevard and Lake Park Drive in the mid-$800s to low-$900s — rare, and possibly already pending. The closest large communities run roughly $1,000–$1,150 for 1-bedrooms (with concessions) and $1,300+ for 2-bedrooms at Apartments at Decker Lake, with newer Decker Station studios starting near $1,075.

Expanding to a ~30-minute drive does unlock better deals for studios and small one-bedrooms, especially in Magna (~$800–$850) and at The Redwood (~$849–$1,069). Absolute cheapest studios (~$650–$799) appear in Tooele, but that commute usually exceeds 30 minutes. If the friend’s workplace near Decker Lake is the anchor, prioritize near-park multiplex bargains first; if rent minimization is the anchor within a half-hour drive, start in Magna and on Redwood Road, then fall back to Decker Lake complexes for convenience.
