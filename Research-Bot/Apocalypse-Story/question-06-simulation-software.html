Simulation Software for Epidemic and Post-Collapse Scenarios

Research report for Apocalypse Story, Question 6 — surveying open-source repositories and academic simulation tools relevant to rapid population loss, epidemic spread, infrastructure failure, and societal breakdown in developed nations (especially the US).

1. Aims of This Document

This report inventories software that could support research into the Apocalypse Story premise: a blood-borne epidemic with a 2–3 day latent period, >50% mortality, and rapid US population reduction over weeks to months — with the focus on societal consequences rather than virology itself.1

Question 6 asks three things:

  1. What open-source repositories exist for simulating these scenarios?
  2. What papers and technical analyses underpin them?
  3. What technology stacks do they use?

The deliverable is two comparison tables — one for open repos, one for academic tools and methods — plus context on how these tools relate to the project's research goals.

Scope boundary. No single open-source project today simulates the full chain from a novel hemorrhagic epidemic through medical-system collapse, law-and-order breakdown, fuel disruption, and long-horizon demographic recovery. Tools cluster into epidemic transmission, infrastructure/disaster resilience, and emergent social/civilization simulation. This report maps those clusters and notes where integration would be required for Apocalypse Story–style modeling.

2. Limitations

2.1 Theoretical limitations

  • Human behavior is chaotic. Panic, rumor, trust in institutions, and culturally specific responses cannot be fully encoded. Even LLM-driven "generative agents" extrapolate from training data, not from unprecedented collapse.23
  • Models are not predictions. George Box's aphorism — "all models are wrong, but some are useful" — is cited repeatedly in epidemic policy tooling; usefulness depends on whether the model illuminates the decision at hand.4
  • Coupling scales is hard. Microscopic agent decisions, regional infrastructure graphs, and national mobility each require different abstractions; hybrid ABM–ODE methods are an active research area, not a solved integration problem.56
  • Collapse is path-dependent. Monte Carlo civilization simulators (e.g., Civilis) explore distributions of outcomes but cannot validate against ground truth for novel scenarios.7

2.2 Methodological and literature limitations

  • COVID-19 bias. A 2023 scoping review of 200+ COVID modeling studies found agent-based models were the most common method, but nearly all target SARS-CoV-2 parameters and NPIs, not hemorrhagic filovirus-like dynamics.8
  • US-centric synthetic populations. FRED and Covasim ship US census-based populations; global tools (GLEAM, STEM) use different demographic pipelines.91011
  • Maintenance drift. Several influential academic codebases (EMOD, public FRED) are archived or superseded by commercial forks; stars and commit dates are not quality metrics.1213
  • Repository discovery. This survey used web search, GitHub organization pages, peer-reviewed software papers, and government/NIST documentation (July 2026). Niche lab tools without public repos are underrepresented.

3. History of the Field

Epidemic simulation evolved from deterministic compartmental ordinary differential equations (Kermack–McKendrick, 1927) through stochastic and network extensions, to individual-based microsimulation on synthetic populations.1422

TIMELINE (simplified) 1927 ─── Kermack-McKendrick SIR compartmental theory 1990s ─── Individual-based / microsimulation (e.g., EpiSimdemics lineage) 2008 ─── EpiSimdemics: 280M-node US contact network on HPC (Barrett et al.) 2011 ─── GLEAMviz released: global mobility + compartmental engine (Broeck et al.) 2013 ─── FRED open-sourced: census synthetic populations (Grefenstette et al.) 2013 ─── STEM/Eclipse: modular spatiotemporal epidemiology (Ford et al.) 2018 ─── EpiModel JSS paper: network ERGM epidemic models in R (Jenness et al.) 2020 ─── COVID-19 modeling explosion: Covasim, OpenABM-Covid19, CovidSim, COMOKIT 2021 ─── Covasim PLOS Comp Bio; Epiabm re-implements CovidSim pedagogy 2023 ─── Starsim generalizes IDM disease suite; hybrid ABM-ODE papers proliferate 2023 ─── Generative Agents (Stanford): LLM-driven social simulation 2024 ─── GEMS (Julia ABM); SAGESim (GPU Python); CDC IXA-EPI-COVID (Rust) 2025 ─── NHERI SimCenter platform paper; pyrecodes LLM household agents

Parallel tracks developed for disaster resilience: NIST's community resilience planning framework (2015+) led to IN-CORE; the NHERI SimCenter (NSF) integrated R2D, pelicun, and pyrecodes for regional natural-hazard workflows.202324

Agent-based modeling platforms (NetLogo 1999, Repast 2000, MASON 2003, Mesa 2015) provided general simulation kernels; epidemic-specific tools increasingly build on Python scientific stacks (NumPy, SciPy, JAX) rather than custom DSLs.2526

4. Conceptual Taxonomy

┌─────────────────────────────────────┐ │ APOCALYPSE STORY RESEARCH NEED │ │ epidemic → care collapse → society │ └─────────────────┬───────────────────┘ │ ┌───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────┐ ▼ ▼ ▼ EPIDEMIC LAYER INFRASTRUCTURE LAYER SOCIAL LAYER (who gets sick/dies) (power, water, transport) (panic, migration, order) │ │ │ Covasim, Starsim, IN-CORE, pyrecodes, Mesa examples, OpenABM, FRED, EMOD, InfraRisk, SIRA, R2D generative_agents, GLEAM, EpiModel, GEMS pelicun GABM-Epidemic, Civilis │ │ │ └───────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────┘ │ GAP: few integrated open pipelines

Three modeling paradigms recur in the literature:1527

  • Compartmental (ODE/PDE): fast, population-aggregated; weak on individual behavior and spatial detail.
  • Agent-based (ABM): explicit individuals, contacts, interventions; computationally expensive.
  • Metapopulation / hybrid: patches or regions as compartments with ABM inside hotspots; increasingly used for scalability.56

5.1 Technology patterns in open repos

  • Python dominance: Covasim, Starsim, Mesa, pyrecodes, InfraRisk, Episia, epimodels — all leverage NumPy/SciPy ecosystems.1026
  • Performance backends: C/C++ (OpenABM, CovidSim, FRED, EMOD, cEpiabm), Rust (IXA-EPI-COVID), Julia (GEMS), GPU+MPI (SAGESim).333435
  • LLM integration (emerging): generative_agents, GABM-Epidemic, pyrecodes household agents — behavior via API calls, not mechanistic psychology.2145
  • Spatial platforms: GAMA/GAML for COMOKIT; Mesa-Geo for hazard ABMs.3743

7. Key Papers and Technical Analyses

Beyond per-tool citations, these cross-cutting analyses inform software choice:

  • Compartmental vs ABM philosophy: Velasco & Casal (2021) compare strengths for prediction, explanation, and intervention — arguing case-by-case preference rather than universal superiority.27
  • ABM platform survey: Abar et al. compared 85 ABM platforms (cited in Li 2025 review) — design guidelines for tool selection.56
  • Computational epidemiology pipeline: Barrett et al. (2021) survey population synthesis, social networks, and simulation software as integrated methodology.22
  • COVID simulation scoping review: Sun et al. (2023) — ABM most frequent among 200+ studies.8
  • Hybrid green computing: Schulte et al. (2024) — up to 98% CO₂ reduction and 50× speedup via metapopulation ABM hybridization.6
  • NHERI platform: SimCenter (2025) — modular open-source natural-hazard workflow as template for reproducible regional simulation.23

8. Mapping Tools to the Apocalypse Story Premise

For a hemorrhagic epidemic with 2–3 day latency, blood-borne spread, >50% mortality, and 5-day fatal course post-symptom onset, researchers would need to reparameterize existing engines rather than find a turnkey model:

Research question (Apocalypse Story)Best starting toolsGap
Speed of infection across US regionsCovasim/Starsim, FRED, GLEAMviz, EpiSimdemics (if accessible)Blood-borne vs respiratory contact networks
Hospital / ICU overwhelmCovasim (built-in hospital layers), RHEAStaff absenteeism, supply chain for blood products
Who dies without care (Q3 overlap)Custom SEIRD compartments; IN-CORE health impactsChronic disease dependency not standard in epidemic ABMs
Power / water / telecom failureInfraRisk, IN-CORE, pyrecodesTrigger = workforce loss, not earthquake
Panic, migration, lawlessness (Q2/Q4 overlap)GABM-Epidemic, Mesa custom models, generative_agentsNo validated "collapse" calibration data
Long-term demographic recoveryFPsim + epidemic module; Civilis (abstract)Fertility/migration feedback under trauma
Recommended integration architecture (not yet implemented as single OSS project): (1) Starsim or FRED for disease + hospital occupancy → (2) export workforce reduction curves → (3) InfraRisk or pyrecodes for infrastructure derating → (4) Mesa or LLM-ABM for behavioral layer. Each coupling is a research project.

9. Conclusion

The open-source ecosystem for epidemic and disaster simulation is rich but fragmented. The COVID-19 pandemic produced a durable foundation of Python ABMs (Covasim → Starsim), Oxford/Imperial microsimulations, and Julia/GPU next-generation frameworks (GEMS, SAGESim). Parallel disaster-resilience stacks (IN-CORE, NHERI SimCenter, InfraRisk, pyrecodes) model infrastructure and recovery but rarely ingest epidemic-driven workforce loss. Emerging LLM-agent models (Generative Agents, GABM-Epidemic) offer a novel behavioral layer at small scale.

For Apocalypse Story programmatic simulation goals, the highest-leverage starting points are:

  1. Starsim or Covasim — reparameterize disease natural history; extract hospital and mortality trajectories.1028
  2. FRED — US census-grounded mixing groups if national heterogeneity matters.9
  3. pyrecodes or IN-CORE — downstream societal/infrastructure consequences once population and workforce shocks are defined.2045
  4. Mesa + custom modules or GABM-Epidemic — exploratory behavior under unprecedented mortality.2126

No surveyed repository implements the full Apocalypse Story chain out of the box. The most credible near-term path is a loosely coupled pipeline rather than a monolithic "collapse simulator."

10. Further Research Questions

  • Which existing synthetic US populations (RTI, FRED, Covasim) best support blood-borne vs aerosol transmission refits?
  • Can hybrid ABM–ODE frameworks (2024 literature) run at US scale with hospital-bed constraints and worker absenteeism?
  • What workforce-to-infrastructure derating functions does IN-CORE or InfraRisk support, and can they be epidemic-triggered?
  • What is the minimum viable coupling between Starsim mortality output and pyrecodes recovery models?
  • How do LLM-agent epidemic models (GABM) compare to rule-based panic models on historical disaster behavior datasets?
  • Are EpiSimdemics contact networks obtainable for non-HPC researchers, and under what licenses?
  • What wargame outputs from Question 5 (government frameworks) reference specific simulators from Table 2?
  • Could Civilis-style Monte Carlo collapse metrics be calibrated against historical population-loss events (Question 1)?
