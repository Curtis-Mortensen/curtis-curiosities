Breakdown of Law and Order After Crisis

How quickly do societies devolve into rule-breaking, looting, and violence — and how does disaster behavior differ from protest and unrest?

Aims and Research Questions

Primary question (assigned)

How quickly does a situation devolve to rule-breaking, looting, and greater violence? What psychological studies and books address this? Do people behave differently in disasters than in protests or unrest? What was learned from BLM protests? What do case studies from developed nations over the past 250 years show?

Precursor and subset questions identified

  1. What counts as "breakdown"? — Looting, violent crime, vigilante justice, police abandonment, prison releases, and elite overreaction are distinct phenomena often conflated in media.[1]
  2. Disaster, catastrophe, or civil disturbance? — Scholars distinguish consensus emergencies (earthquakes) from conflict emergencies (riots); behavior differs systematically.[2]
  3. Mass panic vs. elite panic? — Does the public panic, or do authorities panic about the public?[3]
  4. Survival taking vs. criminal looting? — Taking food and water after infrastructure collapse is often relabeled as looting.[4]
  5. Do crime rates actually rise? — Police data often show decreases, not increases, after disasters.[5]
  6. What role does media play? — Rumors and sensational reporting can create perceived breakdown where verified crime is limited.[6]
  7. What pre-existing conditions predict breakdown? — Inequality, corrupt policing, infrastructure destruction, and delayed aid appear repeatedly.[7]
  8. How fast do riots escalate compared to disasters? — Civil disturbances can reach peak violence within hours; disaster antisocial behavior, when it occurs, often emerges over days.[8]

Limitations of This Research

Theoretical limitations

Human behavior under existential threat is inherently chaotic at the individual level. Aggregated patterns are more predictable than any single person's actions. Social identity, prior grievance, group membership, and perceived injustice interact in ways no model fully captures.[9] The "therapeutic community" observed after many disasters is a statistical tendency, not a law of nature — and it can coexist with pockets of violence, vigilantism, or state repression in the same event.[10]

Definitions of "law and order" are politically loaded. Property crime, survival taking, political property damage, and symbolic looting during riots serve different social functions and are often counted together in public discourse.[11]

Methodological and literature limitations

  • Field study constraints: Disaster researchers conduct "quick response" field studies during or immediately after events, but measuring looting is difficult because rumors outnumber verified incidents by orders of magnitude.[1]
  • Western/developed-nation bias: The classic consensus — that looting is rare in industrialized societies — was built primarily from U.S. and European cases between 1950 and 2000.[12] Recent scholars argue this framework underweights inequality and may not generalize.[13]
  • Media as primary source problem: Many historical accounts of post-disaster violence come from newspapers and official statements later found inaccurate (e.g., Katrina Superdome atrocity stories).[6]
  • Protest data coding: ACLED and similar datasets code "violent demonstrations" to include property destruction and statue toppling, which inflates "violence" figures relative to interpersonal harm.[14]
  • Comparability: A six-day riot, a three-day flood, and a months-long pandemic cannot be compared on a single "hours to breakdown" metric without specifying what broke down.

History of the Field

The study of behavior during disaster and unrest has roots in two competing traditions that only converged in the mid-20th century.

19th century: "The crowd as beast"

Gustave Le Bon's The Crowd (1895) argued that individuals in crowds revert to a primitive, irrational, antisocial nature — a view that influenced military doctrine, journalism, and early sociology for decades.[9] This "breakdown" narrative still appears in disaster films and official planning assumptions.[16]

1950s–1960s: The NORC revolution

Charles Fritz and colleagues at the National Opinion Research Center (NORC), University of Chicago, began systematic field studies after tornadoes, floods, and explosions in the early 1950s.[17] They found cooperation, not panic, dominated. E.L. Quarantelli coined "disaster myths" in this period — the gap between popular belief and observed behavior.[18]

1963: Disaster Research Center founded

Quarantelli, Russell Dynes, and Eugene Haas established the Disaster Research Center (DRC) at Ohio State University — the world's first social-science disaster research center — initially funded by civil defense contracts aimed at understanding wartime civilian behavior.[15] The center moved to the University of Delaware in 1985 and has conducted 650+ field deployments.[19]

1970s–1990s: Consensus vs. conflict framework

Dynes and Quarantelli formalized the distinction between consensus emergencies (disasters) and conflict emergencies (riots, civil disturbances), explaining why looting patterns differ.[2] The 1960s U.S. urban riots provided comparative data alongside hurricane and earthquake studies.

2000s–present: Myths challenged, catastrophes reconsidered

Hurricane Katrina (2005) reignited debate: veteran scholars maintained looting was exaggerated; younger scholars like Kelly Frailing argued Katrina proved the myth framework was incomplete.[20] The "catastrophe" concept (beyond mere "disaster") was refined to explain when antisocial behavior becomes widespread.[21] The 2020 BLM protests and COVID-19 panic buying added new datasets. A 2025 systematic review found mixed evidence on post-disaster looting prevalence.[22]

Executive Overview

Central finding: Law and order does not uniformly or rapidly collapse after crisis. The speed, severity, and type of breakdown depend critically on whether the event is a natural disaster, a catastrophe (total infrastructure/governance failure), or a civil disturbance (organized grievance). In typical developed-nation disasters, prosocial behavior dominates and verified crime often decreases. Widespread looting and violence are far more characteristic of riots and certain catastrophes with pre-existing inequality and state failure.

Psychological and Sociological Research

Disaster myths (Quarantelli, Dynes, Tierney)

Over 50 years of research identifies six persistent myths: mass panic flight, widespread looting, passivity, role abandonment, mass mental breakdown, and belief that outsiders cause problems.[18] In typical Western disasters, 70–90% of survivors hear looting rumors, but verified cases are extremely few.[1] Up to 90%+ of search-and-rescue is performed by civilians, not professionals.[12]

Emergent norm theory

During disasters, new group norms form rapidly — the "therapeutic community" — prioritizing rescue and mutual aid. Property norms temporarily shift: taking necessities may be tolerated; luxury theft is condemned.[2] In riots, norms shift differently: property of symbolic targets becomes fair game to certain subgroups.[2]

Mass panic is rare; collective resilience is common

Modern crowd psychology (Drury, Reicher, Ntontis) finds that emergencies produce affiliative behavior — people seek togetherness, not atomized flight.[9] A 2025 Annual Review concludes that "panic" as popularly understood is largely a myth used to blame victims for crowd crush fatalities caused by venue mismanagement.[30]

Elite panic

Kathleen Tierney, Lee Clarke, and Caron Chess document "elite panic" — authorities' fear of the public leading to militarized response, rumor propagation, property obsession, and deadly force.[3] Rebecca Solnit argues this elite panic often causes more harm than civilian behavior.[16] After Katrina, Mayor Nagin and police chief Compass made inflammatory statements about "hoodlums" and martial law that amplified fear.[25]

Panic buying (COVID-19) — a different breakdown

The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated that "breakdown" need not mean violence. Panic buying of toilet paper and hand sanitizer was driven by perceived scarcity, media amplification, social contagion, and fear of missing out — not lawlessness.[31] Retailer purchase limits and government messaging reduced hoarding.[32] Crime rates in many jurisdictions fell during lockdowns, though domestic violence increased in some areas.[33]

BLM Protests (2020): What Was Learned

George Floyd's murder (May 25, 2020) triggered the largest protest mobilization in U.S. history — over 7,750 BLM-linked demonstrations by August 22, 2020.[14]

Scale of violence vs. peaceful protest

  • 93–95% peaceful: ACLED found fewer than 570 of ~10,600 summer demonstrations involved violence, vandalism, or looting (~5%).[14] A year-long study found ~94% peaceful, 6% with violence/destruction.[29]
  • Geographic concentration: Violent incidents were "largely confined to specific blocks," not movement-wide.[37]
  • Agent provocateurs: Minneapolis "umbrella man" (linked to white supremacist gangs) smashed windows on May 27, helping spark looting after initially peaceful protests.[14]
  • Out-of-town agitators: Detroit officials reported non-residents traveling to protest sites to commit violence.[14]

State response

  • Federal forces deployed to Portland and other cities; National Guard activated in multiple states.[29]
  • Police used force more often at BLM demonstrations than other protests.[14]
  • At least 10,000 arrests nationwide by June 4; LAPD arrested 4,029 May 29–June 2, only 3–5% for looting (most for curfew violations).[29]
  • Dozens of vehicle-ramming attacks on protesters documented.[14]

Academic findings on effectiveness of limited violence

A PNAS study (2022) found mixed violence within BLM protests did not reduce prejudice but increased policy support among conservatives in liberal areas — suggesting disruptive tactics may shift policy views among resistant populations without broad attitude change.[38]

Lesson for apocalypse scenarios: BLM 2020 demonstrates that large-scale civil mobilization in developed nations can remain overwhelmingly non-violent even amid property damage in localized areas — but media coverage, agent provocateurs, and aggressive policing can create a perception of widespread breakdown disproportionate to verified interpersonal violence.

Conclusion

Concise Conclusion

Law and order does not automatically or rapidly collapse when developed nations face crisis. The evidence strongly supports three tiered conclusions:

  1. Typical disasters (earthquakes, hurricanes where infrastructure partially holds): Prosocial behavior dominates; verified looting is rare and often condemned; crime rates frequently fall. Breakdown, when it occurs, emerges over days, not minutes, and is usually localized.[12]
  2. Civil disturbances and riots (Watts, LA 1992, BLM-adjacent violence): Escalation occurs in hours; looting is more collective and selective; behavior is driven by grievance, not survival. Even here, the majority of BLM events were peaceful.[29]
  3. Catastrophes (Katrina flooding, St. Croix, Haiti, Kanto 1923): When the state and infrastructure fail together — especially with pre-existing inequality — antisocial behavior can become widespread within 24–72 hours. Media and elite panic often amplify perceived breakdown beyond verified crime.[3]

For the Apocalypse Story epidemic scenario: a disease causing 50–90% mortality over weeks is closer to a catastrophe than a typical disaster. The relevant question is not "will everyone loot?" but whether institutional collapse, resource denial, and pre-existing inequality convert a consensus emergency into a conflict emergency — and whether elite panic accelerates that conversion.
