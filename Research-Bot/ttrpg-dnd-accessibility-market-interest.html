How Many People Want Accessible TTRPGs? Market Interest Numbers for D&D and Tabletop Role-Playing

A research brief on lifetime play rates, positive experience signals, the dungeon-master bottleneck, and system market share — written for founders building tools that make collaborative storytelling easier to start and keep going.

Research-Bot report · July 2026 · General-audience market research (not investment advice)

1. What This Document Is (and Aims to Do)

This report answers a practical product question: Is there enough public interest in tabletop role-playing games (TTRPGs) — especially Dungeons & Dragons — to justify a product that makes the hobby more accessible? “Accessible” here means lowering the barriers that keep people from playing or from running games: finding a group, finding a Game Master / Dungeon Master (DM), learning rules, and turning a story idea into a playable session.

The document is written so a non-specialist can follow it. You do not need to know how D&D works. Where numbers disagree, the report shows the range and explains why the sources differ. Almost every factual claim is tied to a cited source.

Working thesis. Roughly one-fifth to one-quarter of U.S. adults have tried a TTRPG at least once — close to the ~20% figure from early product surveys. Lifetime D&D exposure alone is in the tens of millions in the U.S. and (by publisher claims) tens of millions worldwide. Direct population surveys of “I had a positive experience” are scarce, but clinical and qualitative research plus high return-to-play rates among past players suggest many tryers leave with goodwill. The clearest commercial bottleneck is not player curiosity — it is Dungeon Master supply: only about 15–20% of players ever run games, while healthy tables need closer to 20–25%, and looking-for-group boards show roughly 5 players hunting a DM for every DM offering seats. D&D dominates market share by a wide margin; Pathfinder, Call of Cthulhu, and a rotating cast of licensed titles fill the next tier.

2. Precursor and Subset Questions

Following Research-Bot practice, the big question was broken into smaller ones researched in order:

  1. Penetration: What share of adults have played a TTRPG (or D&D specifically) at least once? How do “ever played,” “rarely play,” and “actively play” differ?
  2. Absolute scale: What headcounts do publishers and independent surveys imply for the U.S. and the world?
  3. Sentiment: Among people who have tried the hobby, what evidence exists for positive vs. negative experiences?
  4. DM / storyteller gap: What percent of players DM? How many want to DM but do not? Is there quantified demand for more DMs?
  5. Competitive landscape: What is the market share of D&D vs. other TTRPGs (sales and playtime)?

3. Limitations of This Research

3.1 Theoretical limitations

Human hobbies are noisy. Whether someone “has played D&D” depends on memory, social desirability, and what counts as play (one session at a friend’s house vs. a multi-year campaign). Interest in running a game can change with life stage, free time, and who is available to play. Market surveys freeze a moving target.

Self-report ≠ behavior. Saying you like something, saying you would DM, and actually scheduling a session are three different acts. Products that reduce friction test revealed preference better than attitude surveys.

Selection and survivorship. People still in the hobby are easier to survey than people who tried once and left. That biases hobby forums, playtest panels, and “player surveys” toward enthusiasts — and can overstate positivity and understate drop-off.

3.2 Methodological & literature limitations

  • No single gold-standard census of TTRPG players exists. Figures come from (a) publisher / investor claims, (b) commercial omnibus polls, (c) virtual-tabletop telemetry, (d) specialty-store sales trackers, and (e) convenience samples on Reddit and Discord.
  • “Players” is ambiguous. Hasbro and Wizards of the Coast (WotC) have used “players” for cumulative lifetime tryers, active franchise audiences, and digital-account goals in different contexts. [Hasbro 2023; CNBC 2024]
  • The exact question “Had a story idea but never DMed?” does not appear to have been asked in a published, population-representative survey. Proxies (DM share, prep barriers, looking-for-group ratios, paid-DM markets) are used instead and labeled as such.
  • Aggregator sites (e.g. Gitnux compilations) repeat industry stats with uneven verification. Where possible this report prefers primary or near-primary sources and treats aggregators as leads, not proof. [Gitnux 2026]
  • Paid market-research PDFs sometimes publish precise shares (e.g. “48% WotC”) without transparent sampling. Those figures are noted with a weak-evidence tag.

3.3 Strength of the field

Hobby-games market tracking (ICv2 and trade press) is mature for retail sales rankings but weak for household penetration. Academic study of TTRPGs as leisure and as mental-health adjuncts has grown since the mid-2010s but still relies on small samples. Independent pollsters (YouGov, Researchscape/Troy Press, NPD-cited work) provide the best population baselines, yet questions are rarely repeated identically year to year. Overall: moderate evidence for lifetime try rates; weak evidence for a single positive-experience percentage; moderate-to-strong evidence for a structural DM shortage via multiple converging indicators; strong evidence that D&D is the category leader.

4. History of the Fields That Try to Answer These Questions

Publisher headcounts (1970s–present). From the early days of D&D, TSR and later WotC advertised player counts as marketing and investor narrative. Early 1980s claims put worldwide players in the low millions; modern Hasbro materials cite tens of millions. [EN World summary; Hasbro 2023] These numbers are useful as order-of-magnitude signals but are not independent audits.

Hobby trade press & ICv2 (1990s–present). Specialty-game retailers and distributors developed category sales tracking. ICv2’s annual hobby-game estimates and RPG top-title lists remain the standard industry snapshot for North American specialty retail (not Amazon or all DTC). [BoLS / ICv2 2024]

Virtual tabletops & the Orr Report (2014–2021). Roll20’s Orr Group Industry Report measured which systems people actually ran online — a playtime proxy rather than a sales proxy. It showed D&D 5E usually above half of campaigns on that platform before the report was paused. [Roll20 Orr Q2 2021; Roll20 Wiki]

Population surveys (2010s–2020s). As D&D entered mainstream culture (Critical Role, Stranger Things, Baldur’s Gate 3), pollsters began asking ordinary adults about TTRPG play — Researchscape (2019), NPD-cited work (~2022), YouGov frequency questions (mid-2020s), and Troy Press omnibus work (2026). [Researchscape 2019; Gitnux citing NPD; YouGov crosstabs; Troy Press 2026]

DM-shortage discourse. Community talk of “not enough DMs” is decades old; WotC staff publicly described a “rather desperate” need for more DMs around the 5e era, and 2020s product strategy (easier DMG tools, Adventurers League, digital builders) still targets the same bottleneck. [Dicebreaker; AIDungeonMaster analysis] Academic wellbeing research on D&D is newer still (roughly 2020 onward). [Merrick et al. 2024]

5. Finding 1 — How Many People Have Played a TTRPG (1×+)?

Bottom line. Independent U.S. adult surveys cluster around ~16% ever played D&D and ~23–28% ever played any TTRPG, depending on question wording and year. That matches the product team’s informal ~20% signal. Publisher lifetime totals (50M–85M worldwide for D&D) are larger because they include non-U.S. players and multi-decade cumulative tryers.

5.2 What “~20%” means in practice

If roughly 20–25% of U.S. adults have tried a TTRPG once, that is on the order of 50–65 million adults in a ~260M adult population — before counting teens. Researchscape’s 2019 math (23% of ~225M online adults ≈ 52M) is a concrete illustration. [Researchscape] Troy Press’s stricter D&D-only question (16%) implies a smaller but still large tryer base — tens of millions who know the brand from play, not just from media. [Troy Press]

Age skew matters for a product pitch: Researchscape found 45% of men under 45 and 39% of women under 25 had played a TTRPG, vs. single-digit rates among many older cohorts. [Researchscape] YouGov’s never-play rate rises from 51% (18–29) to 85% (65+). [YouGov crosstabs]

5.4 Active play vs. lifetime try

YouGov’s 12% monthly+ TTRPG rate is a much tighter “still in the habit” circle than the ~20–28% lifetime try rates. [YouGov] Community and trade analyses often cite active D&D player estimates in the low tens of millions globally (figures such as ~13.7M circulate in secondary blogs extrapolating older WotC data) — treat those as approximate, not census-grade. [StoryRoll synthesis] For an accessibility product, both layers matter: lifetime tryers are a reactivation pool; monthly players are the core demand for better tools.

6. Finding 2 — How Many Had a Positive Experience?

Honest gap. No large, population-representative survey was found that asks past TTRPG players a clean “Was your experience positive?” and publishes the percentage. The product team should treat any single “X% loved it” claim with skepticism until it fields its own question. What follows are the best proxies.

6.1 Proxies from general-population and past-player work

  • Many tryers are not “never again.” In Troy Press’s 2026 sample of past D&D players, substantial shares reported play in the recent years (including 2024–2026), not only nostalgia play before 2020 — consistent with at least some positive or curiosity-driven return, though not a direct satisfaction score. [Troy Press]
  • Board-game enjoyment is high in the same culture, but that is adjacent entertainment, not TTRPG-specific: YouGov finds 65% of Americans love or like board games, with only 6% dislike/hate. [YouGov] Useful for social-gaming climate; not a D&D NPS.
  • Engaged-player playtests score very high (often 80%+ satisfaction on Unearthed Arcana / One D&D packets), but respondents are self-selected fans who opted into playtesting — upward bias is large. [Wargamer / Crawford]

6.2 Academic and clinical signals (positive effects among participants)

A 2024 peer-reviewed study in Games for Health Journal (Merrick, Li, & Miller) followed 25 community participants through eight weekly hours of D&D. Researchers reported significant decreases in depression, stress, and anxiety and significant increases in self-esteem and self-efficacy over the study period, concluding D&D “may have potential utility as a wellbeing intervention or prevention program.” [Merrick et al.; JCU summary]

Qualitative work (“Roll for Insight”) interviewing regular players found the experience described as primarily positive and rewarding, with themes of escapism, self-exploration, creative expression, social support, and routine. [Baker et al. / ResearchGate] Scoping reviews of therapeutic RPG use similarly catalog growing case evidence, while cautioning that the research base is still early. [Arenas et al. 2022]

Interpretation for product messaging: It is fair to say that among people who stick with play long enough to be studied, experiences skew positive and can support wellbeing. It is not yet fair to claim “X% of the 20% who tried D&D had a great time” without new survey work. A defensible interim framing: large tryer base + strong enthusiast satisfaction + early clinical positives ⇒ substantial latent goodwill, with unknown drop-off among one-session tryers.

7. Finding 3 — Story Ideas, Interest in DMing, and the DM Shortage

Bottom line. Direct data on “I had my own story idea but never DMed” was not found in published surveys. The closest robust picture: ~15–20% of players ever DM (WotC-linked “DMs are ~20% of customers”; community surveys in a similar band), while a typical table needs closer to 1 DM per 4–5 players (~20–25%). Multiple demand signals show the shortage is real: looking-for-group imbalances (~5:1), official acknowledgment of needing more DMs, rising paid-DM marketplaces, and prep/scheduling as top barriers.

7.1 How many players DM?

  • WotC commentary summarized in community analysis: DMs are about 20% of customers — roughly one DM per four players if tables average DM+4. [Seed of Worlds]
  • Secondary industry analyses synthesizing community surveys put “ever DMed” around 15–20%, with a smaller share DMing regularly. [AIDungeonMaster; StoryRoll]
  • Hobby surveys of online communities often show ~50/50 DM/player respondents — a sampling bias (DMs hang out in DM spaces), not population truth. [Seed of Worlds]

7.2 Interest in DMing vs. actually DMing

Older survey archival notes (pulled together by Seed of Worlds) report a striking asymmetry: about 12% of respondents were DMing though they would rather be players, versus only about 3% who were players but preferred to DM. That pattern suggests many who want the DM seat already have it, while a chunk of current DMs are reluctant “forever DMs” — a supply problem driven by duty and scarcity, not by a huge idle queue of eager unused DMs. [Seed of Worlds]

That does not kill the product thesis for accessibility tools. It reframes it: the opportunity may be less “millions secretly want to DM tomorrow” and more (a) reduce burnout among the 15–20% who already carry the hobby, (b) convert a larger share of story-interested players into first-time DMs by cutting prep and rules fear, and (c) serve groups that cannot find any DM at all.

7.4 Mapping to “had a story idea but didn’t DM”

Without a direct survey item, the most honest statement for stakeholders is:

A large majority of D&D players (~80–85%) primarily experience the game as players, not as DMs. Storytelling interest is culturally central to the hobby (actual-play media, homebrew culture), but converting that interest into regular DMing is blocked by prep time, rules confidence, scheduling, and performance anxiety. Quantified “story idea → never DM” conversion rates remain an open research question the product should measure itself.

8. Finding 4 — Market Share of Different TTRPGs

Bottom line. Dungeons & Dragons is the category gorilla across specialty retail sales and major VTT playshare. Pathfinder is the usual #2 in hobby-store RPG rankings. Call of Cthulhu often punches above its retail rank on virtual tabletops. Everything else is a long tail that reshuffles with new licensed releases.

8.1 Specialty retail rankings (ICv2 / trade press)

For 2023, ICv2-linked reporting placed U.S./Canada hobby-game sales around $2.885 billion (+~1% YoY). RPGs as a category softened after COVID highs, but the top RPG titles were still led by D&D, then Pathfinder, Cyberpunk RED, World of Darkness, and Starfinder. [BoLS summarizing ICv2; RPG Drop 2024 analysis]

Industry commentators commonly estimate that D&D products still represent on the order of ~50% or more of RPG dollar sales in specialty channels in recent years — a dominance that survived the 2023 OGL controversy, even as some players temporarily explored alternatives. [RPG Drop]

8.2 Playshare on Roll20 (Orr Report — last detailed public window)

Roll20’s Orr Report Q2 2021 (campaign share on that VTT) is one of the clearest public playtime snapshots before the report went on hold:

8.3 Researchscape system mix among U.S. tryers (2019)

Among Americans who had ever played a TTRPG, Researchscape found roughly six in ten had played D&D, with notable shares also reporting Star Wars RPG (25%) and Star Trek Adventures (17%) — a reminder that licensed games matter in awareness even when they do not lead ongoing sales charts. [Researchscape]

8.4 Vendor market-share claims (use carefully)

Some commercial market reports assign WotC roughly ~48% of player-engagement share and Paizo ~12%, with the rest fragmented. [IndustryResearch.biz claim] Treat these as directional, not audited. They align qualitatively with ICv2 and Orr (D&D first, Paizo second tier) but the precision is not independently verified here.

For a product that “makes TTRPGs more accessible,” the numbers argue that awareness and trial are already mainstream enough; the scarce resource is the ability to start and sustain games — especially the DM role and the logistics around it.

10. Conclusion

Interest is real and large. Multiple independent U.S. surveys put lifetime TTRPG exposure near the team’s ~20% intuition (roughly 16% for D&D-only; low-to-high 20s for any TTRPG). Publisher cumulative figures (50M–85M D&D players worldwide) reinforce that this is a mass-culture franchise, not a niche basement hobby.

Positive experience is plausible but under-measured. Clinical and qualitative research finds benefits and predominantly rewarding play among participants who stick with the game; enthusiast playtests are highly positive. A clean population percentage of “positive first experiences” is still missing.

The DM bottleneck is the strongest demand story for accessibility tools. Only about one in five to one in six players ever DM, looking-for-group markets are chronically one-sided, and players pay real money ($15–25+ per seat) for professional GMs. Whether millions of non-DMs specifically “have a story ready” is unproven in published data — but the structural shortage of facilitators is well supported.

D&D owns the category; Pathfinder and Call of Cthulhu are the main secondary gravity wells, with licensed and indie games rotating through the charts.

Further research questions

  1. Field a population-representative item: “Have you ever played a TTRPG?” → “How positive was it?” → “Have you ever wanted to run a game / tell your own story as DM?” → “What stopped you?”
  2. Measure conversion: among people who consume actual-play media (Critical Role, Dimension 20, etc.), what share have never sat at a table?
  3. Quantify prep-time elasticity: how much does DM willingness rise if prep falls from ~2–3 hours per play hour to <30 minutes?
  4. Segment the 80% non-DM players: forever-players by preference vs. blocked aspirants vs. burned-out ex-DMs.
  5. Compare accessibility tools’ willingness-to-pay against StartPlaying seat prices as a revealed-preference benchmark.
  6. Track system loyalty after OGL / ORC / 2024 rules turmoil — does D&D’s share erode for new cohorts?
