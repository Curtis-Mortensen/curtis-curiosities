Arcosanti & The Cosanti Foundation: What Happened After Paolo Soleri, and Where Things Stand Today

Research report compiled July 8, 2026 — following Research-Bot guidelines

Aims of This Document

This report investigates the current state of Arcosanti — Paolo Soleri's experimental "arcology" town near Mayer, Arizona — and the Cosanti Foundation that operates it. It was prompted by a visitor's conversation with a resident who reported hard times, blamed the post-Soleri foundation, cited roughly 30 full-time residents, and recalled a Not So Native podcast interview about a 2018 grey-water project and a search for partnerships rather than money.

The research aims to:

  1. Trace what changed after Paolo Soleri's death in April 2013.
  2. Identify the 2018 grey-water infrastructure work and whether it was the last major improvement.
  3. Clarify who ran marketing/communications then and who does now.
  4. Map current foundation leadership (board and staff).
  5. Assess resident population claims, including the "~30 residents" figure.
  6. Synthesize the most accurate picture available as of mid-2026.

Executive Summary

Bottom line: Arcosanti is in its most severe organizational crisis since Soleri's death. After decades of slow construction, financial strain, leadership turnover, and reputational damage from abuse allegations against Soleri, the foundation's board reportedly laid off more than 80% of staff in 2025, ended workshops (2024), eliminated volunteers (Fall 2025), terminated on-site ceramic bell production, and ended formal agricultural activities (December 31, 2025).[1] The board is in a year-long "strategic pause" aiming for a new plan by end of 2026.[1]

The podcast you likely remember is Not So Native, Season 4, November 21, 2019 — not 2021 or 2023 — featuring Tim Bell as Director of Communications.[2] Bell is no longer in that role. Marketing/communications has since been held by Kelly Bird (c. 2020), Kate Bemesderfer (2021–2023), and is not clearly filled on the current staff page.[3][4][5]

Your resident's figure of ~30 full-time residents is plausible and consistent with the downward trajectory (official site still claims ~45; tax filings cited ~60 in FY2023; 2019 press reported 70–80).[6][7][8] A complete public roster of all current residents does not exist; only partial names can be assembled from press, alumni communications, and foundation materials.

Limitations

Theoretical / Epistemic Limitations

  • Intentional communities are opaque. Resident grievances, internal board politics, and employment decisions are rarely documented in public filings. Oral testimony (like your conversation) may be accurate but is not independently verifiable.
  • "Discouraging residency" is interpretive. Structural policy changes (requiring employment, ending workshops/volunteers, layoffs) can feel like discouragement without any explicit anti-residency policy.
  • Founder-centric institutions struggle with succession. Soleri's charismatic, top-down model left weak organizational infrastructure — a pattern noted by his daughter Daniela and by former CEO Patrick McWhortor.[9]

Methodological Limitations

  • Research relied on web-accessible sources: foundation website, IRS Form 990 summaries (ProPublica, Cause IQ), Wikipedia (with cited references), Arizona press, national architecture press, podcast archives, and NLRB filings. Paywalled articles (Bloomberg, Daily Independent) could not be fully retrieved.
  • No direct interviews with current residents, board members, or staff were conducted.
  • Foundation website content appears partially stale (e.g., still describing active workshops in some sections while Wikipedia reports cessation).[5][1]
  • Wikipedia's 2025 crisis section cites a When In Your State article (April 2025) that was not retrievable at research time; the claims align with NLRB data and other secondary sources but should be treated as reported rather than independently confirmed.[1][10]

History of the Field: Arcosanti as an "Urban Laboratory"

Paolo Soleri (1919–2013), an Italian-trained architect who studied under Frank Lloyd Wright, coined arcology — architecture + ecology — and founded the Cosanti Foundation in 1965.[11] Ground was broken at Arcosanti in 1970 on 860 acres near Cordes Junction, Arizona, with a visionary target population of 5,000; the built complex remains roughly 5% complete, with the last major structure finished in 1989.[1][15]

For five decades the project's lifeblood was:

  • Workshop labor — paying students/volunteers who built while learning (8,000+ participants by 2024).[1]
  • Windbell sales — bronze and ceramic bells cast on-site via Cosanti Originals.[1]
  • Tourism — tours, events, overnight stays (once ~30,000–40,000 visitors/year).[3]
  • Donations and grants — typically a small fraction of revenue (~$92K–$104K/year in recent 990s).[16]

The experiment always sat between utopian urbanism and practical nonprofit survival — praised for prescient anti-sprawl ideas, criticized for slow building, car dependence, food insecurity, and patriarchal founder culture.[15][17]

2013–2017: Immediate Post-Soleri Period

Jeff Stein, a former Arcosanti workshop participant and Boston architecture dean, had already become foundation president in 2011 when Soleri retired at 92.[1][18] One year after Soleri's death, Phoenix Magazine reported the site could "sustain indefinitely" on windbell and workshop income but wanted growth and investors; the community was only ~5% built after 40+ years.[19]

Stein proposed new apartment buildings, amphitheater canopy, bakery renovation, and model storage — most unbuilt.[1] The Guardian's 2016 profile found Tamura still optimistic about a developer partnership for a hotel/conference center — an idea Soleri himself resisted ("developer starts with D, like devil").[14]

2017–2020: Reputational Crisis and Leadership Change

In November 2017, Daniela Soleri published "Sexual abuse: It's you, him, and his work" on Medium, alleging childhood sexual abuse and attempted rape by her father.[20] She had informed the foundation board in 2010 upon resigning; the board made Soleri step down as chairman privately but issued no public statement until after her 2017 essay.[9] The foundation expressed support for Daniela and pledged to reconsider Soleri's legacy while continuing the work.[21]

Patrick McWhortor, a nonprofit executive, became CEO in 2018 to professionalize outreach; Tim Bell joined communications/community engagement in 2017 and lived on-site from March 2018.[9][3] The Guardian (2020) reported ~80 permanent residents, a third being recent young arrivals, and Convergence festivals drawing diverse crowds largely unaware of abuse history.[9]

2021–2023: Martin-Malikian Era and Resignation

Architect and professor Elizabeth Martin-Malikian became CEO December 1, 2021, with plans to grow operations 10%, expand workshops, and engage 8,000+ alumni.[4] After two years she resigned in September 2023; board chair Matteo Di Michele praised her strategic plan and partnerships. Ivan Fritz, a long-time board member, became interim CEO while the board searched for a permanent replacement.[22]

2024–2026: Contraction and Reported "Mothballing"

David Turnbull, an architect and educator, appears as President & CEO in Cause IQ personnel data (October 2024) and led a February 2025 "Arcology + AI" event and 2025 Alumni Reunion alongside Board Chair Nicole Ackerina.[7][23] Despite this, Wikipedia (updated through 2025) reports that the board laid off 80%+ of staff, ended workshops in 2024, eliminated volunteers in Fall 2025, terminated ceramic bell production, ended agricultural activities December 31, 2025, and entered a strategic pause with vacant Executive Director and Board Chair positions — citing treasurer Stephen Ostwinkle's email to remaining employees.[1]

An NLRB petition filed April 15, 2025 lists 16 employees in a bargaining unit covering guest services, archives, maintenance, café, and alumni/workshop staff — suggesting a workforce already much smaller than the 28 employees reported in FY2023.[10]

The 2018 Grey-Water Project

The 2018 grey-water work is well-documented and was among the last visible infrastructure improvements on-site.

What Was Built

  • East Crescent grey-water system: The foundation's desert-water page describes installing a grey-water system in the East Crescent neighborhood to reuse sink and shower water for landscape irrigation — part of broader water conservation (low-flush toilets, rainwater harvesting, aquifer recharge).[13]
  • ASU EPICS partnership: Arizona State University's Engineering Projects in Community Service program partnered with Arcosanti on grey-water reclamation (without black water), aiming to purify water for a small orchard — described as exploratory research with no prior existing examples.[12]
  • Earlier context: A 2016 Guardian Cities piece photographed students installing a grey-water recycling tank on-site while Jeff Stein discussed slow but continuing growth.[14]

Was 2018 the Last Major Improvement?

Largely yes for capital infrastructure. No new major buildings have been completed since 1989.[1] Post-2018 activity focused on programming (Convergence festivals, alumni events, tours, workshops), land stewardship mapping, and organizational changes rather than construction. The Greenhouse Apron, underway before Soleri's death, was put on hold after 2013.[11] Wikipedia notes formal agricultural activities ended December 31, 2025.[1]

How Residency Works

Historically, the path to residency was the 5–6 week workshop. Currently (per website), residency requires employment by the Cosanti Foundation or Cosanti Originals — not all jobs require living on-site.[6] With workshops ended and volunteers eliminated, fewer pathways to residency exist, which may explain residents' perception of being discouraged even without an explicit anti-residency policy.

"Discouraging People from Living There"

Your resident's claim could not be confirmed in any published foundation policy, press release, or board statement. However, several structural changes produce a similar effect:

  1. Employment-only residency: You can no longer become a resident through the classic workshop pathway; you must be hired.[6]
  2. Workshops ended (2024): Removed the primary pipeline that converted visitors into long-term community members.[1]
  3. Volunteers eliminated (Fall 2025): Removed another residency pathway.[1]
  4. Mass layoffs (2025): Fewer jobs = fewer resident slots; NLRB shows only 16 employees in the bargaining unit.[10]
  5. Low wages: Tim Bell noted in 2019 that residents leave because nonprofit salaries cannot fund retirement; community size itself drives attrition.[8]
  6. Stagnation sentiment: A 2019 resident told Cronkite News: "It's been quite stagnant… people are comfortable with how it's been."[8]

The foundation may be pivoting from "living experiment" to "heritage site in strategic review" — which long-term residents could reasonably experience as being pushed out or devalued, even if no one said "we don't want you here."

Revenue is overwhelmingly from program services (~85%) — tours, bells, events — not donations. The organization has run operating deficits in recent years while net assets declined from ~$5.45M to ~$4.77M.[16] Windbell sales and tourism were never sufficient to fund Soleri's full vision; the 2025 contraction suggests the board concluded the operating model was unsustainable at prior staffing levels.

Partnerships vs. Money

The "we need partners more than money" theme appears across eras:

  • 2016: Tamura discussed a developer partnership for hotel/conference center to fund expansion.[14]
  • 2018–2020: McWhortor emphasized rebuilding external outreach and addressing reputational damage while defending the mission.[9]
  • 2021–2023: Martin-Malikian built "strategic partnerships with complementary organizations," academic conferences, and alumni engagement.[22][4]
  • 2025: "Arcology + AI" symposium with ASU-affiliated speakers suggests continued appetite for intellectual partnerships.[23]
  • Board committee structure: Development Committee (David Fordon, chair) and Education Committee (Nadia Begin, chair) are explicitly open to public involvement — signaling partnership-oriented governance.[5]

Your resident's intuition that the site has uses is shared by many alumni and urbanists; the constraint has been organizational capacity, funding, permitting, and leadership continuity — not lack of imaginable purposes.

Conclusion

Arcosanti remains standing — the concrete vaults, apse, foundry, and windbells are real — but the organizational experiment may be at its lowest ebb since Paolo Soleri's death. The Cosanti Foundation has cycled through multiple CEOs, faced reputational harm from abuse revelations, never solved the economics of building a 5,000-person city at 5% completion, and — per multiple sources — drastically reduced staff and programs in 2025 while the board searches for a new direction through 2026.

Your resident's experience aligns with documented trends even where specific claims ("they're discouraging us") lack paper trails. The ~30 resident count is believable. The podcast was Tim Bell in 2019, not 2021/2023, and he no longer holds that role. The 2018 grey-water project was real and among the last infrastructure wins. The foundation's leadership today is a mix of long-arc veterans (Stein, Begin, Ostwinkle, Hoadley, Tamura) and newer professional board members (Adams, Fordon, Lynn), with David Turnbull as the most recently identified CEO — though his status during the 2025 layoffs is unclear.

The site still has extraordinary potential as an educational landmark, retreat, research station, or partnership hub. What it lacks is a stable institutional model that connects that potential to the people who live there.
