Management Theory & Organizational Science

Research Report for Management Sim — Question 1: What are the main theories of business and organizational management, how has the field evolved, what does an MBA teach, and what does research say about good vs. poor management?

Compiled July 2026. Self-contained document; no prior context required.

1. Introduction and Aims

This document surveys the intellectual foundations of business and organizational management — the theories, books, curricula, and empirical research that underpin how modern firms are designed, led, and operated. It is written to inform a management simulation video game in which players make structural, personnel, and strategic decisions and observe their consequences on productivity, morale, innovation, and survival.

Central thesis: Management is neither pure art nor pure science, but a contingent applied discipline that has evolved through five broad eras — pre-industrial craft, classical efficiency, human relations, systems/contingency, and data-driven modern practice. No single "best way" to manage exists; outcomes depend on fit between organizational design, leadership behavior, workforce capability, and environmental turbulence. However, decades of rigorous research confirm that management quality is a first-order determinant of firm performance — often larger than capital, technology, or industry effects within a given competitive set (Bloom & Van Reenen, 2007; Lazear, Shaw & Stanton, 2015; Gallup, 2015).

The research questions addressed:

What are the main theories of business and organizational management? What are the most influential books? How has management evolved historically? What does a typical MBA curriculum cover? What does science say about effective management and leadership? What are ongoing debates — especially around flat organizations and the reduction of middle management? What studies demonstrate the difference between good and poor management in achieving outcomes?

This report addresses those questions through eight lenses: (1) precursor and definitional questions, (2) limitations and evidence strength, (3) the history of management as a field, (4) major theoretical schools, (5) leadership theory, (6) the MBA curriculum, (7) influential books, (8) empirical research on management quality and organizational design, and (9) implications for game design.

2. Precursor and Subset Questions

Before surveying theories, several definitional and scope questions must be resolved. Each shapes what "management" means in both scholarship and gameplay.

Management vs. Leadership

Kotter (1990) distinguished management (planning, budgeting, organizing, controlling) from leadership (setting direction, aligning people, motivating). Mintzberg (1973) found managers spend most time in interpersonal, informational, and decisional roles — not the rational planning model textbooks describe. For a game, both are levers: structural design (management) and inspirational behavior (leadership).

Organization vs. Firm

Management theory applies to any coordinated group — corporations, hospitals, armies, NGOs, startups. Theories differ in emphasis: classical theory targeted factories; contingency theory addressed diversified conglomerates; modern theory includes platform firms and remote teams (Daft, 2015).

Universal vs. Contingent

Classical and "best practice" schools (Taylor, Fayol, Peters & Waterman) claim universal principles. Contingency theory (Lawrence & Lorsch, 1967; Burns & Stalker, 1961) argues optimal structure depends on environment, technology, and size. This tension is central to game design: should one strategy dominate, or should context matter?

Prescriptive vs. Descriptive

Many canonical texts are prescriptive ("do this"). Academic research is increasingly descriptive and causal (randomized trials, natural experiments). A simulation should distinguish player advice (which may be wrong) from ground-truth mechanics (what research supports).

Outcome Metrics

"Good management" can mean productivity, profitability, survival, employee wellbeing, innovation rate, or ethical conduct. Different theories optimize different outcomes. Research typically measures productivity, profitability, Tobin's Q, survival, and employee engagement (Bloom & Van Reenen, 2007; Gallup, 2015).

Level of Analysis

Theories operate at individual (motivation), team (group dynamics), organizational (structure), and institutional (industry, regulation) levels. A management sim must decide which levels are player-controllable and which are environmental constraints.

3. Limitations of This Research

3.1 Theoretical Limitations

Organizations are open, adaptive systems. Unlike physics, management lacks universal laws. Findings from manufacturing plants in the 1920s may not transfer to software teams in 2026 (Lawrence & Lorsch, 1967; Perrow, 1970).

Publication and survivorship bias. Famous case studies (GE, Toyota, Enron) are cherry-picked. "Excellence" books profile successful firms retrospectively; many "excellent" firms later failed (Byrne, 2003; Rosenzweig, 2007).

Western and corporate bias. Most canonical theory originates in American and European industrial contexts. Cross-cultural management (Hofstede, 1980; House et al., 2004 GLOBE study) reveals significant variation in authority, communication, and motivation norms.

Leadership is endogenous. Firms that are already succeeding attract and retain better managers, confounding causality. Randomized experiments (Bloom et al., 2013 India RCT) are rare but growing.

Management fashion cycles. TQM, reengineering, Holacracy, and "radical transparency" rise and fall. What is "cutting edge" in one decade becomes discredited or absorbed into baseline practice in the next (Abrahamson, 1996).

3.2 Methodological and Literature Limitations

Management research relies heavily on surveys, case studies, and correlational analyses. The World Management Survey (WMS) pioneered structured interview scoring but still measures self-reported practices (Bloom & Van Reenen, 2007). Laboratory leadership studies lack ecological validity. Historical accounts of Taylor, Mayo, and Drucker are often hagiographic. MBA curricula vary enormously across schools; this report synthesizes US-centric full-time programs (HBS, 2025; Wharton, 2025; AACSB, 2024).

4. History of Management as a Field

4.1 Pre-Industrial and Proto-Management (Ancient–1800)

Organized administration predates industrial capitalism. Sun Tzu's Art of War (circa 5th century BCE) addressed strategy, logistics, and morale. Kautilya's Arthashastra (circa 3rd century BCE) described state bureaucracy, taxation, and espionage. Roman military organization, Venetian merchant guilds, and the Dutch East India Company's joint-stock governance all practiced proto-management — but without systematic theory (Wren & Bedeian, 2018).

Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations (1776) introduced division of labor and pin-factory specialization — ideas later operationalized by Taylor. The Industrial Revolution (1760–1840) created the factory system, urban wage labor, and the management problem at scale: how to coordinate hundreds of workers producing standardized goods (Chandler, 1977).

4.2 Classical Management Era (1880–1940)

Frederick Winslow Taylor (1856–1915) pioneered scientific management at Midvale Steel and Bethlehem Steel. His 1911 Principles of Scientific Management argued every task has "one best way," discovered through time-and-motion study, with workers selected and trained scientifically and managers responsible for planning while workers execute (Taylor, 1911; Wren & Bedeian, 2018). Taylorism spread globally, influencing Ford's assembly line and Soviet Stakhanovite campaigns — but also provoked worker resistance and union opposition (Kanigel, 1997).

Henri Fayol (1841–1925), a French mining executive, articulated administrative theory in General and Industrial Management (1916, English 1949). He defined five functions — planning, organizing, commanding, coordinating, controlling (POCCC) — and fourteen principles including unity of command, scalar chain, and esprit de corps (Fayol, 1949). Where Taylor focused on the shop floor, Fayol addressed the entire organization.

Max Weber (1864–1920) analyzed bureaucracy as the rational-legal ideal type: hierarchical authority, written rules, impersonal relationships, specialized roles, and merit-based appointment (Weber, 1922/1947). Weber saw bureaucracy as technically superior but warned of an "iron cage" of rationalization. Together, Taylor, Fayol, and Weber form the classical school — efficiency, hierarchy, and rationality (Wren & Bedeian, 2018).

Parallel contributions: Frank and Lillian Gilbreth advanced motion study and ergonomics; Henry Gantt developed the Gantt chart and incentive wage systems; Harrington Emerson promoted efficiency engineering (Wren & Bedeian, 2018).

4.3 Human Relations and Behavioral Era (1924–1970)

The Hawthorne Studies (1924–1933) at Western Electric's Hawthorne Works near Chicago transformed management thought. Initially testing lighting's effect on productivity, researchers (including Elton Mayo from Harvard Business School) found that social attention, group norms, and supervisory style mattered as much as physical conditions (Roethlisberger & Dickson, 1939; Baker Library, HBS). The "Hawthorne effect" — improved performance from being observed — became legendary, though later scholars argue the effect was overstated and methodologically flawed (Parsons, 1974; Wickström & Bendix, 2000).

Regardless of methodological disputes, the human relations movement shifted focus from task efficiency to social psychology. Abraham Maslow (1943) proposed the hierarchy of needs. Douglas McGregor (1960) contrasted Theory X (workers are lazy, need coercion) with Theory Y (workers are self-motivated, seek responsibility). Frederick Herzberg (1959) distinguished hygiene factors from motivators. Chris Argyris (1957) critiqued formal organizations for creating employee immaturity (Wren & Bedeian, 2018).

Chester Barnard's Functions of the Executive (1938) bridged classical and behavioral thought, arguing organizations are cooperative systems held together by communication and shared purpose — a precursor to systems theory (Barnard, 1938).

4.4 Quantitative and Operations Era (1940–1970)

World War II accelerated operations research: linear programming (Dantzig), game theory (von Neumann & Morgenstern), and statistical quality control (Shewhart, Deming). Post-war, management science applied mathematical models to production, inventory (EOQ), and decision analysis. Herbert Simon (1947) introduced bounded rationality — decision-makers satisfice rather than optimize. James March and Herbert Simon's Organizations (1958) founded organizational behavior as an academic discipline (March & Simon, 1958).

4.5 Systems and Contingency Era (1950–1980)

General systems theory (Bertalanffy, 1950) and open systems models (Katz & Kahn, 1966) treated organizations as entities exchanging resources with environments. Joan Woodward (1965) found technology type (unit, mass, process production) correlated with optimal structure. Tom Burns and G.M. Stalker (1961) distinguished mechanistic (stable environments) from organic (dynamic environments) systems.

Paul Lawrence and Jay Lorsch (1967) demonstrated that firms in turbulent environments require greater differentiation between departments and stronger integrating mechanisms — the foundational contingency theory finding. Jay Galbraith (1973, 1977) developed information-processing models of organization design. Henry Mintzberg (1973, 1979) empirically described managerial work as fragmented, interrupted, and reactive — challenging rational planning models (Mintzberg, 1973).

4.6 Strategy, Quality, and Excellence Era (1980–2000)

Michael Porter's Competitive Strategy (1980) introduced industry structure analysis (five forces) and generic strategies (cost leadership, differentiation, focus). Tom Peters and Robert Waterman's In Search of Excellence (1982) sold millions, identifying eight attributes of "excellent" companies — though many profiled firms later underperformed (Byrne, 2003). W. Edwards Deming and Joseph Juran brought Japanese-quality lessons to the West; Total Quality Management (TQM) became a global movement. James Womack and Daniel Jones (1990) popularized lean manufacturing from Toyota Production System studies (Womack, Jones & Roos, 1990).

Peter Drucker (1909–2005) — the most prolific and influential management thinker — wrote 39 books including The Practice of Management (1954), The Effective Executive (1967), and Innovation and Entrepreneurship (1985). He coined "knowledge worker," "management by objectives," and "privatization," and advised leaders from Alfred Sloan to Jack Welch (Beatty, 2016; Collins, 2009).

Jim Collins and Jerry Porras' Built to Last (1994) and Collins' Good to Great (2001) studied long-run corporate performance. Clayton Christensen's Innovator's Dilemma (1997) explained disruptive innovation. Peter Senge's Fifth Discipline (1990) promoted learning organizations.

4.7 Modern Era: Data, Agility, and Post-Hierarchy (2000–present)

Management science has become increasingly empirical. The World Management Survey (Bloom & Van Reenen, 2007) quantified management practices across 35 countries. Google's Project Oxygen (2008–) used internal data to identify effective manager behaviors. Gallup's engagement research linked managers to 70% of engagement variance (Gallup, 2015). Agile, OKRs (Doerr, 2018), and platform/ecosystem strategy (Parker, Van Alstyne & Choudary, 2016) reshaped practice.

Simultaneously, a counter-movement pushed flat organizations: Zappos' Holacracy experiment (2013–2020), Spotify's squad model, Valve's handbook, and Elon Musk's directives at Tesla and SpaceX to bypass hierarchy and delete middle management (Hsieh, 2010; Musk, 2018; Groth, 2011). Research suggests flat structures improve idea generation but risk execution failures without alternative coordination mechanisms (Lee & Edmondson, 2021; SI Labs, 2026).

Post-COVID, remote and hybrid work management, AI-augmented decision-making, and debates over stakeholder capitalism (Business Roundtable, 2019) continue reshaping the field (Bloom et al., 2021; De Cremer et al., 2025).

5.3 Quantitative / Management Science

1940–present Optimization Data

Applies mathematics, statistics, and modeling to management decisions: linear programming, queuing theory, simulation, forecasting, inventory optimization. Simon's bounded rationality acknowledges real managers cannot optimize but satisfice (Simon, 1947). Directly relevant to simulation game mechanics — resource allocation, queuing, bottlenecks.

5.4 Systems Theory

1950–present Holism Environment

Organizations are open systems — they import inputs (capital, labor, materials, information), transform them, and export outputs (products, services, waste) while adapting to environmental feedback (Katz & Kahn, 1966). Subsystems (production, marketing, HR) must be integrated. For a game: environmental events (regulation, competition, supply shocks) are core system inputs.

5.5 Contingency Theory

1960–present Fit Context

There is no one best way to organize. Optimal structure depends on environment (Burns & Stalker, 1961), technology (Woodward, 1965), size (Blau, 1970), strategy (Miles & Snow, 1978), and information-processing needs (Galbraith, 1973). This is perhaps the most important design principle for a management sim: player strategies should succeed or fail based on environmental fit.

5.6 Organizational Culture and Strategic Management

Edgar Schein (1985) defined culture as shared basic assumptions. Porter's industry analysis (1980, 1985), the resource-based view (Barney, 1991), and dynamic capabilities (Teece et al., 1997) address competitive advantage. Deming, Juran, Ohno, and Womack/Jones provide the quality/lean toolkit for operations gameplay.

6.2 Transformational Leadership (Detail)

Bass (1985) operationalized four factors: idealized influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, and individualized consideration. Meta-analyses find positive correlations with performance and satisfaction (Judge & Piccolo, 2004), though heroic-leader bias and endogeneity are concerns (Tourish, 2013).

6.3 What Managers Actually Do (Mintzberg, 1973)

Observational study of five CEOs: managers work at relentless pace, favor verbal communication, and perform interpersonal (figurehead, leader, liaison), informational (monitor, disseminator, spokesperson), and decisional (entrepreneur, disturbance handler, resource allocator, negotiator) roles. This contrasts with MBA classroom ideals — a tension worth modeling in gameplay.

7.6 What the MBA Does and Does Not Teach

The MBA provides analytical frameworks (finance, strategy, operations), a professional network, and a credential signal. It teaches about management more than how to manage — case discussions simulate decisions but cannot replicate the interpersonal complexity Mintzberg documented. Research on MBA value is mixed: career earnings premiums exist especially at elite schools (GMAC, 2024), but evidence that MBA training directly improves managerial performance is weak (Pfeffer & Fong, 2002; Rosenzweig, 2007).

9. What Research Says: Good vs. Poor Management

This section synthesizes the strongest empirical evidence linking management quality to outcomes — the quantitative foundation for simulation mechanics.

9.1 World Management Survey (Bloom & Van Reenen, 2007+)

The most ambitious attempt to measure management scientifically. Researchers developed an interview-based scoring tool rating 18 practices from 1 (worst) to 5 (best) across three dimensions (Bloom & Van Reenen, 2007; Bloom et al., 2012):

DimensionWhat "Good" Looks LikeWhat "Bad" Looks Like
MonitoringContinuous tracking of performance, systematic data collection, regular reviewNo meaningful metrics; performance invisible until crisis
TargetsStretching but achievable goals; cascaded from strategy; reviewed regularlyNo clear targets; goals too easy or impossible; no follow-up
People / IncentivesPerformance-based promotion and pay; underperformers managed (train or exit); talent retainedPromotion by tenure; no consequences for poor performance; talent leaves

Key findings from 14,000+ firms across 35 countries (Bloom et al., 2012; Sadun, Bloom & Van Reenen, 2017):

  • Management scores strongly correlate with productivity, profitability, Tobin's Q, sales growth, and survival (Bloom & Van Reenen, 2007).
  • US firms score highest on average; a long tail of extremely poorly managed firms exists in every country.
  • Poor management is more prevalent when product market competition is weak and when family firms pass control to eldest sons (primogeniture) (Bloom & Van Reenen, 2007).
  • Management practices account for ~30% of cross-country productivity differences — comparable to R&D and IT (Bloom et al., 2012).
  • Better-managed firms adapted faster to remote work during COVID-19 (Bloom et al., 2021).

9.2 Randomized Controlled Trial: India (Bloom et al., 2013)

The gold-standard causal test. Researchers randomly assigned 28 Indian textile plants to receive five months of management consulting (implementing WMS practices) or no intervention (Bloom et al., 2013, Quarterly Journal of Economics).

OutcomeResult
Productivity (first year)+17%
Quality defectsReduced
InventoryReduced
Annual profitability+$300,000+ per plant
Within-firm spilloversPositive — practices spread to non-treated plants

Indian firms averaged WMS score 2.69 vs. 3.33 for US firms. Treated plants adopted better monitoring, target-setting, and incentive systems. This provides direct causal evidence that management practices are a production input, not merely a correlate of success.

9.3 The Value of Bosses (Lazear, Shaw & Stanton, 2015)

Using granular output data from a technology-based services firm, Lazear et al. estimated individual supervisor effects on team productivity (Journal of Labor Economics, 2015):

  • Replacing a boss in the bottom 10% with one in the top 10% increases team output by more than adding one worker to a nine-person team.
  • Workers assigned to better bosses are less likely to leave the firm.
  • The average boss adds approximately 1.75× the output of the average worker — consistent with pay differentials.
  • Boss quality is not simply "being the best worker" — the highest-performing individual contributors do not necessarily make the best managers (Caliendo, Monte & Rossi-Hansberg, 2015; Benson, Li & Shue, 2019).

9.4 Gallup: Managers and Engagement (2015)

Gallup's meta-analysis of employee engagement across 2.7 million employees and 105,000 managers (Gallup, 2015):

  • Managers account for at least 70% of the variance in employee engagement scores across business units.
  • Only 30% of US employees (13% globally) are engaged at work.
  • Approximately 1 in 10 people possess natural management talent; organizations routinely promote the wrong people.
  • Engagement correlates with productivity, profitability, turnover, safety, and customer satisfaction.

9.5 Google Project Oxygen (2008–present)

Google initially questioned whether managers mattered. Data from 10,000+ observations proved they do (Garvin, 2013; Google re:Work, 2024). Ten behaviors of effective Google managers:

  1. Is a good coach
  2. Empowers team without micromanaging
  3. Creates inclusive team environment
  4. Is productive and results-oriented
  5. Is a good communicator (listens and shares)
  6. Supports career development and discusses performance
  7. Has clear vision/strategy for team
  8. Has key technical skills to advise team
  9. Collaborates across organization
  10. Is a strong decision maker

Project Aristotle (2016) added that psychological safety — the belief one won't be punished for mistakes — is the top predictor of team effectiveness (Duhigg, 2016; Rozovsky, 2015).

10. Ongoing Debates: Flat Organizations and the "Death of Middle Management"

10.1 The Trend

Since the 1980s, large firms have systematically reduced hierarchical layers and increased spans of control (span = direct reports per manager). Fortune 500 CEO spans rose from 4.5 in the mid-1980s to ~10 by the mid-2000s; organizational depth between CEO and division managers fell ~25% (Rajan & Wulf, 2006; Guadalupe, Li & Wulf, 2014). Post-2020 layoff waves disproportionately targeted middle managers — dubbed the "Great Flattening" (Gallup, 2025). Amazon, Meta, Google, and others publicly discussed reducing management layers to cut costs and accelerate decisions.

10.2 Elon Musk's Approach (Tesla, SpaceX)

Musk's 2018 email to Tesla employees articulated a philosophy now widely cited (Musk, 2018; CleanTechnica, 2018):

"Communication should travel via the shortest path necessary to get the job done, not through the 'chain of command.' Any manager who attempts to enforce chain of command communication will soon find themselves working elsewhere. A major source of issues is poor communication between departments. The way to solve this is allow free flow of information between all levels."

Musk's broader management toolkit includes:

  • First principles thinking — decompose problems to fundamental truths, rebuild from scratch (Musk, 2014 TED).
  • Five-step algorithm (2020): (1) Question requirements, (2) Delete parts/processes, (3) Simplify and optimize, (4) Accelerate cycle time, (5) Automate — in that order (Minnaar, 2020).
  • Flattening management — periodic reorgs to cut "non-value-add layers" (Musk, 2018).
  • Extreme vertical integration — internalize supply chain and production to control quality and speed (Vance, 2015).
  • Mission-driven intensity — long hours, high expectations, tolerance for failure in pursuit of ambitious goals.

Results are mixed by metric: SpaceX and Tesla achieved extraordinary engineering milestones, but also face lawsuits over workplace conditions, high executive turnover, and production chaos during scaling (Wong, 2022; Minnaar, 2020). The approach works best in mission-critical, engineering-intensive, founder-led contexts — less clearly transferable to mature, diversified, or highly regulated organizations.

10.3 Arguments For Flattening

  • Speed — fewer approval layers accelerate decisions (Sah & Stiglitz, 1986; The Conversation, 2018).
  • Cost — McKinsey estimates 10–15% managerial cost savings from rightsizing spans and layers (McKinsey, 2017).
  • Autonomy — frontline employees gain decision authority, increasing motivation (Theory Y alignment).
  • Information fidelity — fewer hierarchical filters reduce distortion (Galbraith, 1973).
  • Innovation — flatter structures improve idea generation (Lee & Edmondson, 2021).

10.4 Arguments Against / Risks

  • Execution failures — "The myth of the flat start-up" (Lee & Edmondson, 2021, Strategic Management Journal): flatter structures improve creativity but cause "haphazard execution" and decision overload.
  • Scaling limits — people can only manage so many direct reports effectively; beyond ~15–20, coaching and coordination break down without structure (McKinsey, 2017; Gallup, 2025).
  • Informal hierarchy emerges — Holacracy and similar experiments at Zappos (2013–2020) found that eliminating formal hierarchy often produces worse informal hierarchy (Groth, 2011; Reingold, 2016).
  • Middle managers as glue — they translate strategy to operations, develop talent, and maintain engagement (Floyd & Wooldridge, 1997; D'Innocenzo et al., 2016).
  • Senior leader overload — removing middle management dumps coordination burden on executives (Vanishing Middle Management, 2024).
  • Career paths — fewer management layers reduce promotion opportunities, affecting retention (Caliendo et al., 2015).

10.6 Research Consensus on Flat Organizations

Current research consensus: The question is not "hierarchy vs. no hierarchy" but "what kind of structure, for what task, at what scale?" Flat structures work for small, creative, fast-moving teams with high-trust cultures and strong coordination alternatives (Agile, OKRs, transparent information systems). They fail when they simply remove managers without replacing their coordination, coaching, and accountability functions. The optimal design is contingent — and the trend toward flattening is as much about cost-cutting and technology-enabled coordination as it is about ideological commitment to empowerment (Lee & Edmondson, 2021; McKinsey, 2017; Gallup, 2025).

11.2 Design Principles

  1. No universal optimum. Contingency theory demands that different strategies win in different environments — stable manufacturing vs. turbulent startup vs. regulated healthcare.
  2. Management is a production input. The India RCT justifies treating management investment as directly increasing output, not just a flavor choice.
  3. Managers matter more than most inputs. Boss quality effects rival adding headcount; engagement variance is 70% managerial.
  4. Structure has costs and benefits. Flattening saves money and speeds decisions but risks execution, burnout, and informal hierarchy.
  5. Include folklore and reality. Players may believe Hawthorne effect, Theory X, or "excellence" attributes work — sometimes they're right, sometimes wrong (Rosenzweig, 2007).
  6. Time horizons matter. Cost-cutting reorgs boost short-term metrics; talent drain and culture damage appear later.
  7. MBA knowledge as skill tree. Finance, strategy, operations, leadership as unlockable competencies mirroring real curriculum.

12. Conclusion

Management theory has evolved over 150 years from Taylor's stopwatch to Bloom's randomized trials — from prescriptive "one best way" doctrines to contingent, empirically grounded understandings of how organizations function. Seven conclusions stand out for game design:

  1. Management quality is causal, not cosmetic. The India RCT and WMS provide strong evidence that how firms are managed directly affects productivity and survival.
  2. Individual managers are high-leverage. Boss quality effects rival headcount decisions; Gallup's 70% engagement finding makes the direct manager the most important single variable.
  3. Context determines optimal design. Contingency theory is the best-supported framework for a sim where no single strategy always wins.
  4. Flat is not free. Removing middle management saves cost and speeds decisions but creates execution, coordination, and talent-development risks — unless alternative structures replace what managers did.
  5. The MBA curriculum maps to game skill trees. Finance, strategy, operations, leadership, analytics, and ethics are natural progression systems.
  6. Popular management wisdom is often wrong. Rosenzweig's halo effect, Hawthorne mythology, and "excellent company" retrospection provide rich material for player misjudgment and learning.
  7. The field is actively evolving. AI management, remote work, stakeholder capitalism, and the Great Flattening are live debates — not settled history.

12.1 Further Research Questions

  • How do management practices diffuse within and between firms? (Bloom et al., 2013 spillover findings)
  • What is the optimal span of control by task type, industry, and manager quality? (McKinsey, 2017; Gallup, 2025)
