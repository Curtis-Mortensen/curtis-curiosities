Combat Balance Guide

A step-by-step reference for designing balanced combat encounters. Covers the core math, three monster archetypes (Standard, Heavy Hitter, Acid), and trade-offs when rounding stats to clean half-point increments.

Overview

This guide describes how to build monsters that produce predictable, fair combat for a party of 2 or 3 players. Most encounters are tuned to last 3 rounds on average (lifesteal types use 4) and leave the party at a specific HP percentage when the fight ends.

Each encounter has a unique Encounter ID — a six-digit code: party size (3 digits) + level (1 digit) + archetype (2 digits). Party-of-2 fights use IDs starting with 002; party-of-3 fights use 003. Level 1 is the easiest and level 5 is the hardest. The last two digits identify the monster archetype: 11 Standard, 12 Heavy Hitter, 13 Acid, 14 Group Attack, 15 Lifesteal, 16 Splitter. For example, party of 2, level 1, standard = 002111; the same fight as a splitter = 002116.

What "level" means here Level 1 = party ends at 60% HP. Each successive level drops that target by 25 percentage points (60% → 35% → 10% → −15% → −40%). Negative values mean the monster is expected to overkill on paper — the party would be knocked out if the fight went the full distance without intervention. However, players have heal abilities, so a win is still theoretically possible at these levels. They are meant to feel very challenging, not unwinnable.

Dice & Hit Chance

Monster attacks are not free-floating percentages — they come from actual dice rolls. Each attack rolls one die (any standard D&D die: d4, d6, d8, d10, d12, or d20) with a +/− modifier, then checks whether the result meets or beats the player's AC of 4.

Attack roll (meets or beats)
Roll 1dX with modifier (+/− X), where X is 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, or 20.
(die result + modifier) ≥ 4 → hit.

Equivalent: the die must show ≥ (4 − modifier) on its face.
Example: 1d10 vs AC 4 No modifier means the die needs to show ≥ 4. Faces 4–10 succeed (7 of 10). Probability = 7/10 = 70.00%.

Assumptions

These baseline stats are held constant across all calculations in this guide.

StatPer PlayerParty of 2Party of 3
HP 4 8 12
Damage per turn (avg) 0.75 1.5 2.25
Combat duration 3 rounds (avg)
Monster HP — 4.5 7.0
Why 0.75 damage per player? Party damage is modeled as a mix: some members hit for 1.0, others for 0.5, averaging to 0.75. Monster HP and damage are always expressed in clean 0.5 increments. When a duo encounter splits HP unevenly (e.g. 2.0 + 2.5), that's intentional — both values are valid half-steps. This constraint sometimes forces compromises on hit chance (see Alternatives).

Core Formulas

Monster HP (how much the party must chew through)

Monster HP
Monster HP = Party Damage × 3 rounds (rounded to nearest 0.5)
  = (Party Size × 0.75) × 3 → round to 0.5

If the party deals 1.5 dmg/turn (size 2), the monster needs 4.5 HP to die in exactly 3 rounds.

Damage the monster must deal (HP target)

Target damage dealt
Dmg Dealt = Party HP − Remaining HP
  = Party HP × (1 − Target Remaining %)

Standard monster: damage per turn

Standard archetype
Target Dmg/Round = Dmg Dealt ÷ 3
Actual Dmg/Round = Base Damage × Monsters × Hit%
Hit Chance = Target Dmg/Round ÷ (Base Damage × Monsters)

Example: need 1.7333 dmg/round with Base 2.0 → ideal hit 86.67% → 1d8+2 at 87.50% gives Actual/Rd = 1.7500 (exact over 3 rounds).

Pick a Base Damage (rounded to the nearest 0.5 step), then solve for the hit chance that makes Actual Dmg/Round closest to the target. Standard monsters aim for 70% or higher hit chance.

Heavy hitter: high damage, low accuracy

Heavy hitter archetype
Same formula as standard, but target hit chance = 43–57% (ideally ~50%).
Higher base damage compensates for missing more often.

Acid damage: two hits per successful attack

Acid archetype
Each successful hit deals Base Damage now + Base Damage next turn (acid tick).

Over 3 attack rounds:
  • Round 1 hit → immediate dmg + acid on round 2
  • Round 2 hit → immediate dmg + acid on round 3
  • Round 3 hit → immediate dmg only (acid would land after combat)

Expected total = 5 × Base Damage × Hit Chance
Hit Chance = Dmg Dealt ÷ (5 × Base Damage)

Acid monsters deal less per swing but get a "free" follow-up hit. The ×5 multiplier (instead of ×3) means they need lower base damage — but also makes low-level tuning harder.

Group attack: damage to every player

Group attack archetype
3 × Hit% × (Dmg per player) × Party Size = Target Dmg Dealt
Per-player damage is lower than standard base damage — the party size multiplier does the work.

Lifesteal: 4-round fights with fixed healing

Lifesteal archetype
Heal on hit = 0.5 HP (fixed — does not scale with damage dealt)
Expected healing = 4 rounds × Monsters × Hit% × 0.5
Listed HP = Standard HP + Party Dmg − Expected Healing − Difficulty
  • Standard HP = Party Dmg × 3 (same baseline as other archetypes)
  • Extra round = +1 × Party Dmg (fight lasts 4 rounds, not 3)
  • Difficulty = 0.5 at 75% hit, +3 per 10% above 75% (slightly harder than neutral)

Example @ 75% hit, party of 2: 4.5 + 1.5 − 1.5 − 0.5 = 4.0 HP
Example @ 85% hit, party of 2: 4.5 + 1.5 − 1.7 − 0.8 = 3.5 HP

Splitter: divide at 50% HP

Splitter archetype
Total HP pool is simulated (lower than standard) because damage splits across bodies.
At 50% max HP, one monster becomes two (each ≥ 0.5 HP). Levels 4–5 start with 2 → 4 bodies.

Design Workflow

  1. Determine party size (2 or 3) and difficulty level (1–5).
  2. Look up the HP target — what % of party HP should remain?
  3. Calculate Dmg Dealt — how much total damage must the monster(s) inflict?
  4. Set Monster HP — always Party Dmg × 3 (split between 2 monsters at levels 4–5).
  5. Choose archetype — Standard, Heavy Hitter, or Acid.
  6. Pick Base Damage in 0.5 increments, then solve for ideal hit chance.
  7. Assign a dice roll — choose the single 1dX±M roll whose hit % is closest to the target.
  8. Validate — hit chance within target range? Damage output matches? Rounds = 3?
Two monsters at levels 4 and 5 At the hardest two levels in each block, encounters use 2 monsters instead of 1. Total HP and total base damage are split evenly between them. Each monster has half the HP and half the base damage, but both attack each round — so combined output is unchanged.

Heavy Hitters

Slow, devastating attackers. They swing hard but miss often. Target hit chance is 43–57% (centered around 50%). Same HP targets and 3-round duration as standard monsters — only the damage/hit trade-off changes.

Design feel Heavy hitters create swingy, tense fights. Players see big numbers but also lots of misses. A lucky streak can wipe the party; a miss streak gives breathing room.

Acid Damage Monsters

These monsters deal damage in two pulses: immediate impact plus an acid tick on the following turn for the same amount. Over a 3-round fight, each monster gets 3 attack opportunities but only the first two also produce acid ticks within combat.

Acid damage timeline (single monster, 3 rounds)
Round 1: [hit?] → immediate dmg + schedule acid
Round 2: [acid from R1] + [hit?] → immediate dmg + schedule acid
Round 3: [acid from R2] + [hit?] → immediate dmg only

Expected output = 5 × Base × Hit%  (not 3 × Base × Hit%)
Lower base, higher hit — but not always Acid's ×5 multiplier means monsters need less base damage than standard types. Hit rates still come from real dice rolls — see the dice table for all achievable percentages.

Lifesteal Monsters

Combat lasts 4 rounds on average. On every successful hit, the monster heals 0.5 HP — regardless of how much damage it dealt. Listed HP is set lower than a standard monster to account for that healing; the penalty scales with hit chance (more hits = more healing = lower starting HP).

Lifesteal HP & healing (4-round fights)
Heal on hit = 0.5 HP (fixed)
Expected healing = 4 × Monsters × Hit% × 0.5
Listed HP = Standard HP + Party Dmg − Expected Healing − Difficulty
Difficulty = 0.5 at 75% hit, +0.3 per 5% above 75% (tuned slightly harder than neutral)

Tune attack damage and hit chance normally (4 × Base × Hit% ≈ Target Dmg Dealt). HP follows from the hit%.
Why HP varies with hit chance A monster that hits 85% of the time heals more over 4 rounds than one at 75%, so it needs less listed HP to stay balanced. At 75% hit the offset is about −0.5 from standard; at 85% it's about −1.0. Duo monsters heal twice as fast (two attackers), so their per-monster HP drops accordingly.

Splitter Monsters

When a monster drops to 50% HP, it splits into two copies (each at half current HP, minimum 0.5). Levels 1–3: 1→2 bodies. Levels 4–5: 2→4 bodies.

Splitter HP (simulated)
Total HP pool is lower than standard monsters because splitting spreads party damage.
Assumption: party damage is split evenly across all living bodies each round.
Split only occurs when half of current HP is ≥ 0.5. Pools tuned to ~3 rounds via simulation.
Split trigger Levels 1–3: 1 monster splits into 2. Levels 4–5: 2 monsters each split into 2 (4 total).

Alternatives & Trade-offs

The tables above represent the recommended stat blocks. Each hit percentage is the closest match from the dice table — the best balance of clean numbers, target hit rates, and accurate damage output. In practice, the 0.5-increment constraint and competing goals often leave you choosing between imperfect options. This section documents those forks.

Guiding principle Target hit chances (70%+ standard, 43–57% heavy, 70%+ acid) are ideals, not hard laws. A stat block that hits 64% but deals exactly the right damage is often better than one that hits 70% but overkills by 10%.

Standard Monsters — Base Damage Rounding

When converting raw monster damage to a 0.5-increment base, the spreadsheet uses: round up to the nearest 0.5, then bump further if hit chance exceeds ~87% (single monster) or ~80% (duo encounters). This keeps hit rates in a satisfying 70–87% band.

Party Damage: 0.75 Average vs. Mixed 1.0 / 0.5

Party damage of 0.75 assumes a mix of attackers (some deal 1.0, some 0.5). This affects monster HP because HP = party dmg × 3.

Party Dmg ModelParty 2 HPParty 3 HPEffect
All members @ 0.75 4.5 7.0 Baseline — current tables
All members @ 1.0 6.0 9.0 Monsters live 1 extra round on avg — fights run long
All members @ 0.5 3.0 4.5 Monsters die faster — fights run short
One @ 1.0, rest @ 0.5 (2-player) 3.75 — Slightly shorter than 0.75 avg

If your actual party composition differs from the 0.75 assumption, adjust monster HP proportionally. The damage/hit tuning for HP targets stays the same.

Acid at Duo Levels — High Hit Rates

When acid encounters split into 2 monsters (levels 4–5), per-monster base damage drops and required hit chance climbs above 90%. This is mathematically correct but may feel like "auto-hit" acid stacks. Options:

  • Accept high hit rates — acid's delayed damage is dodgeable/healable in your game system, so guaranteed application isn't as scary as immediate damage.
  • Increase base, lower hit — e.g., Party 2 Level 4 with 1.5 base each (total 3.0) gives 61% hit. Deals same damage on average but more swingy.
  • Reduce acid tick to 0.5× base — changes the formula to expected = 3×base×hit + 2×0.5×base×hit = 4×base×hit. Opens more tuning room but changes the archetype feel.
